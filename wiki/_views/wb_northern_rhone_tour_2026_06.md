# Northern Rhône Tour — deep dive (Berserkers #120248)

Deep read of the Hall-of-Fame thread [*Northern Rhône Tour: Clusel-Roch, Jamet, Perret,
Gonon, Sorrel, Cave de Tain, Clape, Allemand*](https://www.wineberserkers.com/t/northern-rhone-tour-clusel-roch-jamet-perret-gonon-sorrel-cave-de-tain-clape-allemand/120248)
(orig. 2016-04-21). Parent catalogue: [[wb_hall_of_fame_2026_06#northern-rhone-tour|WB Hall of Fame §3]].

**The setup.** A group of California Rhône-style winemakers did a week-long, deep cellar
tour of the N. Rhône, organized by **Paul Gordon of Halcón Vineyards** after a conversation
with Rhône authority **John Livingston-Learmonth** — the explicit goal being to get the
California and Northern Rhône communities to know each other. The pace was brutal/serious:
**~100 wines a day over four days** (9am vineyard → ~1pm cellar → picnic → afternoon visits
to 7pm → 8:15 dinner in Tain). On **day 5 the Californians poured *their* wines for the
French growers** — and that comparison later became a blind tasting (see bottom).

---

## 10 key takeaways

1. **Whole-cluster is the house style of the north's reference domaines.** The traditional
   benchmarks visited — Jamet (Côte-Rôtie), Clape (Cornas), Sorrel (Hermitage) — all ferment
   substantially or entirely **whole-bunch**, treating stem tannin as structural backbone for
   longevity, not something to fear. This is the through-line connecting the trip's "greats."

2. **New oak is conspicuously absent at the top.** The classic addresses age in **old foudres,
   demi-muids and used barrels** — Clape famously sends *new* foudres to Domaine Ott in Bandol
   to be "broken in" so no fresh-oak flavour ever reaches the wine. The lesson for CA Rhône
   makers: structure and perfume come from site + stems + old wood, not toast.

3. **Blending across many parcels beats single-vineyard isolation — for some.** Jamet builds
   Côte-Rôtie from **~25 parcels across up to 17 lieux-dits**; Clape blends his Cornas terroirs
   (La Côte, Reynard, etc.) into one wine. Complexity is engineered through *assembly*, a
   deliberate philosophical contrast to the New-World single-vineyard reflex.

4. **The artisans are worried about Guigal/Chapoutier-scale vineyard engineering.** A recurring
   thread concern: the big houses are bulldozing and rebuilding hillside sections **without
   terracing and not organically**, raising erosion and identity questions on these fragile
   slopes. The small growers see it as a threat to what makes the north special.

5. **Hand-farming the slopes is non-negotiable and brutal.** Clusel-Roch farms organically,
   **ploughs by hand**, and uses a **winch to drag the plough up the steepest rows** — a
   concrete illustration of why these wines cost what they do and can't be mechanized.

6. **Cornas is the trip's emotional core, and it's about personality as much as place.**
   Allemand "held court," sizing the group up with a sly grin; the self-made-from-nothing
   biography (see below) frames Cornas as the north's most personal, least corporate appellation.

7. **St-Joseph has a clear reference point: Gonon.** The Gonon brothers are repeatedly framed
   as *the* benchmark for the appellation — proof that "lesser" northern AOCs deliver
   greatness in the right hands, at a fraction of Hermitage/Côte-Rôtie money.

8. **A great co-op belongs in the same conversation as the artisans.** Including **Cave de Tain**
   (vinifies ~half the northern Rhône's appellation volume) alongside Clape and Jamet signals
   that scale ≠ mediocrity when parcel selection is taken seriously — a useful corrective to
   small-grower romanticism.

9. **California's best held their own — but not by mimicry.** In the blind sequel the top two
   slots went to **California** (Wind Gap, Halcón) ahead of Jamet and Gonon. The takeaway isn't
   "CA beat France"; it's that **cool-site, restrained CA Syrah plays in the same register** as
   the N. Rhône classics — the exact "sense of place + tension" axis Evan cares about.

10. **Even the legends drink "lesser" appellations young and the grands crus with age.** The
    implicit buying logic across the visits: St-Joseph (Gonon) and Cornas (Allemand, Clape
    younger-vine "Renaissance") for earlier drinking and value; Hermitage (Sorrel Le Gréal) and
    Côte-Rôtie (Jamet) as the long-haul cellar bottles.

## Producer insights

### Clusel-Roch — Côte-Rôtie *(in vault: [[../producers/clusel_roch|Clusel-Roch]])*
Organic; **hand-ploughing with a winch on the steepest rows**. Tiny production of the top
cuvée — **~200 cases of Les Grandes Places**; La Viallière is the other flagship lieu-dit.
Massale-selection mindset (old-vine Serine clones). Reference for *delicate, perfumed* Côte-Rôtie
rather than power. Blind sequel: 2012 Viallière placed 10th of 16 (showing young/closed).

### Jamet — Côte-Rôtie
The thread's archetypal classicist. **~8.5 ha across ~25 parcels in up to 17 lieux-dits**,
majority on the schist of the **Côte Brune** (fractured, well-drained), ~25% Côte Blonde.
**Whole-cluster**, resisted the destemming trend his whole career; complexity comes from the
**interplay of many parcels**, not single-parcel isolation. The 2012 Côte-Rôtie ranked **3rd**
in the blind — the top-finishing French wine. *(No vault page yet — onboarding candidate.)*

### André Perret — Condrieu & St-Joseph
The trip's Condrieu (Viognier) reference, also a serious St-Joseph producer (**Les Grisières**
placed 7th in the blind). Included to show the north's white side and that St-Joseph
red/white can run with the famous reds. *(No vault page yet.)*

### Gonon — St-Joseph *(in vault: [[../producers/domaine_pierre_gonon|Domaine Pierre Gonon]])*
Brothers **Jean & Pierre Gonon**, joined the family domaine 1986/1988, now **the benchmark of
St-Joseph**. Work out of two cramped ancient cellars plus a **new cave finished 2002**.
Old-vine Serine, whole-cluster, restrained. The 2013 St-Joseph ranked **4th** blind — i.e. a
"village" appellation finishing ahead of Hermitage and Cornas grands noms. Single best
value-greatness argument of the trip.

### Marc Sorrel — Hermitage *(in vault: [[../producers/marc_sorrel|Marc Sorrel]])*
Old-school Hermitage. **Le Gréal** = fruit from **Le Méal** (old vines, a majority planted
**1927–28**), ~95% Syrah + ~5% Marsanne co-ferment; **whole-cluster, used oak, 18–24 months**.
**Les Rocoules** is the great white (Marsanne-dominant, ~50-yr vines). Tiny, traditional,
ageworthy — the antithesis of polished modern Hermitage.

### Cave de Tain — co-op (Hermitage / Crozes / St-Joseph / St-Péray)
Founded 1933; **~310–360 grower-members, ~1,000 ha — roughly half of all northern-Rhône
appellation volume** (incl. ~25% of Hermitage). Invested in **new facilities in 2014 to
multiply parcel selections**. The trip's argument that a serious co-op deserves a seat at the
table. *(No vault page yet.)*

### Auguste Clape — Cornas
The Cornas standard-bearer. **No destemming (100% whole cluster), native-yeast ferment in
concrete, no temperature control, ~22 months in old foudres/demi-muids, no new oak (new casks
broken in at Domaine Ott, Bandol), unfined/unfiltered.** The flagship **blends all the
terroirs** (La Côte, Reynard…); **"Renaissance"** is the younger-vine cuvée. The 2013 Cornas
placed **11th** blind and "was guessed incorrectly by most of the group" — a reminder that the
greatest traditional Cornas can show closed/idiosyncratic young. *(No vault page yet —
high-priority onboarding candidate.)*

### Thierry Allemand — Cornas *(in vault: [[../producers/allemand|Thierry Allemand]])*
The trip's most vivid character study. Arrived in Cornas **as a teenager with no wine
background**, apprenticed with **Robert Michel** and did side work with **Noël Verset**,
gradually accumulated vines, **first vintage 1982**; now **~4 ha, ~1,200 cases**. Cuvées
**Chaillot** and **Reynard**. "Held court… eyed each player up and down… a flash of a sly
grin… an interloper who arrived with nothing and built everything with his own hands."
The 2012 Chaillot placed **9th** blind.

## The blind sequel: 2012 N. Rhône vs. 2013 California
Follow-up thread: [*Rhône trip revisited: California vs. Rhône blind tasting*](https://www.wineberserkers.com/t/rhone-trip-revisited-california-vs-rhone-blind-tasting/132977)
— Paul & Jackie Gordon, 16 wines, 16 tasters (winemakers + writers). Final order:

| # | Wine |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2013 Wind Gap, Nellessen Vineyard (Sonoma Coast) 🇺🇸 |
| 2 | 2013 Halcón, Alturas (Yorkville Highlands) 🇺🇸 |
| 3 | **2012 Jamet, Côte-Rôtie** 🇫🇷 |
| 4 | **2013 Gonon, St-Joseph** 🇫🇷 |
| 5 | 2013 Drew, Perli Vineyard (Mendocino Ridge) 🇺🇸 |
| 6 | 2013 Ojai, Solomon Hills "Special Bottling" (Santa Maria) 🇺🇸 |
| 7 | **2013 André Perret, St-Joseph Les Grisières** 🇫🇷 |
| 8 | 2013 Big Basin, Rattlesnake Rock (Santa Cruz Mtns) 🇺🇸 |
| 9 | **2012 Allemand, Cornas Chaillot** 🇫🇷 |
| 10 | **2012 Clusel-Roch, Viallière Côte-Rôtie** 🇫🇷 |
| 11 | **2013 Clape, Cornas** 🇫🇷 |
| 12 | 2012 Qupé, Sawyer-Lindquist (Edna Valley) 🇺🇸 |

**Reading it:** the young French classics (esp. Clape) showed closed and ranked low — exactly
what whole-cluster, old-foudre wines built for 20+ years do at age 3–4. The CA wins reflect
*plushness-on-release*, not superiority over a drinking life. Don't over-read the order; read
the **register overlap** — cool-climate CA Syrah and N. Rhône are now genuinely comparable.

## Cellar takes

- **Double community consensus.** Every classic on this trip (Jamet, Gonon, Clape, Allemand,
  Sorrel, Clusel-Roch) also carries a deep dedicated *fil* on LPV — see
  [[lpv_signal_map_french_regions_2026_06|the LPV signal map]]. Berserkers + LPV agreement = high conviction.
- **Onboarding priorities (no vault page yet):** **Jamet** and **Auguste Clape** first
  (reference Côte-Rôtie + Cornas), then **André Perret** (Condrieu/St-Joseph) and a note on
  **Cave de Tain** as a value benchmark.
- **Value-greatness picks that fit Evan's taste:** **Gonon St-Joseph** and **Allemand Cornas**
  (both already in-vault) are the trip's clearest "tension + sense of place" buys; Clape
  "Renaissance" is the younger-vine entry to the appellation.
- **CA cross-reference:** Wind Gap, Halcón, Drew, Big Basin (cool-site CA Syrah) sit in the same
  register — worth a look against the restrained-California lane.
