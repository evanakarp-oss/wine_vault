# Heymann-Löwenstein

Heymann-Löwenstein is among the most important natural-wine pioneers in Germany and represents the vanguard of Mosel winemaking committed to biodynamic farming, minimal-intervention cellar practices, and the expression of volcanic terroir through completely dry Riesling of remarkable purity and mineral density.

## The Transition to Biodynamics

Heymann-Löwenstein is owned and managed by **Bert Selbach**, whose family has farmed vineyards in Winningen (Lower Mosel) since the 1960s. Selbach inherited conventional vineyards but grew increasingly dissatisfied with synthetic pesticide use and the compromises it entailed. In the 1990s, he began a philosophical and practical shift toward **organic farming**, completing the transition by 2000 and subsequently earning Demeter biodynamic certification — a rigorous standard requiring elimination of synthetic chemicals, moon-cycle planting calendars, biodynamic compost preparations, and soil-building practices.

This was a countercultural move in the 1990s-2000s Mosel. The region was dominated by conventional wine production; organic/biodynamic advocates were rare and viewed as idealistic outsiders. Selbach's choice to pursue Demeter certification was driven by conviction rather than market advantage — indeed, it made the winery economically precarious during the early 2000s when conventional wine was cheaper and easier to sell.

## Volcanic Terroir & The Lower Mosel

Heymann-Löwenstein's vineyards are planted on **volcanic slate and red stone soils**, distinct from the slate-dominant geology of the Middle and Upper Mosel. Winningen sits at a particular confluence point where the Mosel's steep terrain, volcanic subsoils, and Lower Mosel climate converge to create exceptional conditions for Riesling. The volcanic mineralogy imparts a distinctive spice, white pepper, and almost peppery aromatics to the wines — a signature that marks great Lower Mosel Riesling.

The Lower Mosel has historically been overlooked in favor of the Middle Mosel's prestigious sites (Zeltingen, Urzig, Bernkastel). Selbach's success in making world-class wine from this region helped rehabilitate the Lower Mosel's reputation.

## Winemaking Philosophy: Dry Riesling & Minimal Intervention

Heymann-Löwenstein is **exclusively dry-wine focused** — a significant philosophical position given that much traditional German winemaking embraces residual sugar.

**Fermentation & Cellar Approach:**
- **Wild yeast fermentation** — no inoculation; Selbach relies on ambient microbes to ferment the must completely to dryness
- **Extended lees contact** — wines remain on gross lees for months, developing tertiary complexity and autolytic richness
- **Old-oak aging** — uses large neutral casks (500-1,200 liters) rather than new oak, preserving fruit purity while allowing controlled oxygen exchange
- **Unfined & unfiltered** — wines are not clarified; they remain cloudy initially, settling naturally over time
- **Minimal sulfite addition** — sulfites are added sparingly, relying on the wine's own resistance to oxidation and microbial spoilage

**Philosophy Behind the Method:** Selbach's approach reflects natural-wine ideology — the belief that wines made with minimal intervention and without laboratory correction reveal deeper truth about their origin. In practice, this means accepting some cloudiness in youth, occasional "funk" or reductive character that resolves with aeration, and the risk of bottle variation. The payoff is wines of extraordinary complexity, purity, and ageability.

## Key Vineyard Sites & Wines

Heymann-Löwenstein works several notable Winningen parcels:

1. **Röttgen** — the flagship vineyard; steep volcanic slope; wines show white pepper, stone fruit, mineral smoke; ages beautifully for 20+ years
2. **Uhlen** — another prestige site; slightly warmer exposure; richer fruit character but maintained dry-wine discipline
3. **Hammerstein** — steeper terrain; more austere, mineral-driven expressions

Wines typically carry vintage and parcel designation (e.g., "2019 Röttgen"), and Selbach avoids commercial classifications like "Kabinett" or "Spätlese" — all wines are dry, and quality is indicated through the vineyard name and vintage rather than sugar content.

## Production Scale & Sustainability

Heymann-Löwenstein produces roughly 80,000 bottles annually across all cuvées — relatively small for a professionally-managed estate, yet larger than micro-boutique operations. The modest scale allows Selbach to maintain personal oversight of all aspects from vineyard to bottle.

The biodynamic certification requires annual audits and adherence to strict protocols (no synthetic inputs, soil testing, livestock integration where possible). The certification is not a marketing claim but a genuine commitment to regenerative agriculture.

## Critical Standing & Market Position

Heymann-Löwenstein has achieved significant critical acclaim:
- **Wine Advocate / Parker ratings:** Regular reviewer coverage with strong scores (90+) for top vintages
- **CSW documentation:** Multiple articles dating to 2009, with consistent praise for the dryness, minerality, and ageability
- **Natural wine community:** Recognized as a founding figure in German natural wine movement
- **Auction presence:** Older vintages (2000s-2010s) appear regularly at auction with strong sell-through, indicating collector confidence in aging potential

The winery has not become fashionable in the way some natural-wine producers have; instead, it maintains a steady following among serious German-wine collectors and those seeking dry, mineral-driven Riesling.

## US Importation & Availability

Distributed through Skurnik and Terry Theise networks. Retail presence is selective, primarily through independent retailers in major markets with strong European wine portfolios.

## Cellar Fit & Taste Alignment

**Excellent fit.** Heymann-Löwenstein exemplifies all key taste criteria:
- **Biodynamic-certified:** Demeter certification reflects genuine commitment to regenerative farming
- **Terroir-driven:** Volcanic Mosel terroir is expressed through minimal intervention, dry-wine discipline, and extended aging
- **Grower-scale:** Small production, personal oversight, traditional methods
- **Ageability:** Wines demonstrate 25-30+ year aging potential; 2000s bottlings are currently drinking beautifully

The dry-wine focus aligns with Evan's preference for restrained German Riesling (as distinct from sweet-wine traditional production).
