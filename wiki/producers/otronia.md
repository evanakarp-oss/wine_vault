# Otronia

Winemaker: **Juan Pablo Murgia (Grupo Avinea)**. Chubut, Atlantic Patagonia (Patagonia). Category: **Emerging**.

## Approach

50 organic hectares in southernmost Patagonia — the world's most southerly commercial vineyard. Part of the MatrizViva project. Head winemaker Juan Pablo Murgia, who described the 45th parallel latitude as 'conditions that create positive qualities in wine.' The 2025 vintage was exceptional despite 20% lower yields from strong Atlantic winds — Malbec 14.3% ABV with 11g/L total acidity and pH 3.1.

## Grapes

Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Riesling

- Otronia Chardonnay

## Notes

**Otronia** represents an extraordinary geographical and viticultural experiment: operating 50 organic hectares at the 45th parallel in Atlantic Patagonia (Chubut, Argentina) — the world's southernmost commercial vineyard. The producer embodies a provocative thesis: that wine quality in the Southern Hemisphere benefits from extreme latitude (cool climate, extended growing season, high acidity retention), and that Patagonia's Atlantic-facing exposure creates distinctive terroir potential despite being considered marginal for commercial viticulture.

**Extreme Terroir & 45th Parallel Latitude.** The 45th parallel location (equidistant from the Equator and South Pole) creates conditions of intense competition between ripening and acidity preservation. Head winemaker Juan Pablo Murgia (of Grupo Avinea/MatrizViva collective) describes the latitude as creating "conditions that create positive qualities in wine" — a deliberate understatement for what is actually a radical geographical claim. Otronia's position as the world's southernmost commercial vineyard validates the hypothesis that ultra-cool-climate viticulture can produce distinctive, age-worthy expressions.

**Climate Challenge & Terroir Response.** Atlantic Patagonia's climate is notoriously challenging: strong Atlantic winds, limited growing season, and lower solar intensity compared to inland Mendoza. The 2025 vintage reports 20% lower yields due to wind damage, illustrating the site's capriciousness. Yet this adversity creates advantage: the wines that survive these conditions develop lower potential alcohol (Malbec at 14.3% ABV is restrained even for Argentina) and naturally high acidity (11 g/L total acidity, pH 3.1 — values that would be extraordinary outside cool-climate contexts).

**Organic Farming & MatrizViva Collective.** The 50-hectare holding is certified organic, reflecting a philosophical commitment to sustainability and environmental stewardship in a remote, ecologically sensitive region. Otronia is part of the MatrizViva (Living Matrix) collective, a grouping of Patagonian producers exploring the region's potential through coordinated research, shared resources, and collective marketing. This collaborative approach reflects a broader Patagonian ethos: individual producers are less important than the region's collective discovery.

**Key Wines & Varietal Expressions.** The flagship is **Pinot Noir 45 Rugientes** (45 Fierce Winds) — a direct reference to the Atlantic gales that define the site. The name itself is a marketing statement: these are wines shaped by elemental forces, not by winemaker manipulation. Additional expressions include **Chardonnay** and **Malbec** — varietals chosen to explore how extreme latitude transforms their classic profiles. Each wine carries the 45th parallel signature: lower alcohol, crisp acidity, distinctive mineral profile.

**Emerging Producer Status & Recognition.** Tagged as "Emerging" in the vault, Otronia has garnered attention from progressive critics (Decanter, James Suckling 2025) and sommeliers exploring Argentina's frontier regions. The producer appears in Argentina Reloaded events, positioning it as part of the curator's vision for Argentina's future-oriented producers. Wildman importation suggests US distribution through natural-wine, adventurous-focused retail channels.

**Patagonian Significance & Climate-Change Implications.** Otronia represents a provocative geographical shift in wine production: as climate change makes traditional regions hotter and drier, extreme-latitude sites become increasingly valuable for producers seeking high acidity, lower alcohol, and classical terroir expression. Otronia's success at the 45th parallel suggests that Argentina's southernmost frontier may emerge as a source of genuinely distinctive, age-worthy wine — not merely a curiosity but a serious response to changing global wine geography.
