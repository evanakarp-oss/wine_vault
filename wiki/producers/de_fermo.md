# De Fermo

Contemporary Abruzzo biodynamic estate founded in 2010 by Stefano Papetti Ceroni (former lawyer from Bologna) and Eloisa de Fermo. Located in Loreto Aprutino, De Fermo revived a family farm that had ceased wine production in 1955, replanting 17 hectares of vineyard across a broader 200-hectare property.

## History & Philosophy

Stefano Papetti Ceroni, a Bologna-trained lawyer, discovered winemaking and commitment to biodynamic farming, leaving his legal practice in 2015 to become full-time producer. De Fermo represents a contemporary return to the land and to traditional Abruzzo farming practices, informed by modern biodynamic understanding.

## Vineyard & Property

17 hectares of grapevines on a 200-hectare estate encompassing olive groves, vegetable gardens, herb cultivation, legumes, and grazing land for cattle. The diversified farm reflects a holistic biodynamic vision: wine integrated into a living agricultural ecosystem. Vineyard breakdown: 10 hectares Montepulciano (primary focus), 3.5 hectares Pecorino, 2.5 hectares Chardonnay, under 1 hectare Trebbiano.

## Winemaking Approach

Whole-bunch fermentation with indigenous yeasts; no temperature control or other interventions to shape the result. Minimal sulfur dioxide use. 18th-century cellar with large wooden barrels and concrete vats; deliberately eschews modern technology in favor of age-tested, gentle methods. Biodynamic calendar informs vintage decisions. The philosophy: allow natural processes to express the vineyard's character without winemaker interference.

## Key Wines

- **Montepulciano d'Abruzzo** — the estate focus; multiple bottlings and ages
- **Pecorino d'Abruzzo** — white varietal, representing the secondary focus
- **Chardonnay** — small production
- **Trebbiano d'Abruzzo** — minimal bottling

## Regional Context

De Fermo represents a new generation of Abruzzo producers bringing contemporary biodynamic practices and natural-wine philosophy to the region, while honoring its traditional techniques (wooden barrels, whole-bunch fermentation, minimal intervention). The estate's diversified agricultural approach connects wine production to the broader ecosystem and cultural landscape of rural Abruzzo.

## Notes

De Fermo exemplifies a contemporary European trend: professionals (often urban professionals) returning to family agricultural land with renewed commitment to natural farming and traditional winemaking. Stefano's transition from lawyer to biodynamic farmer mirrors similar movements in Burgundy, Piedmont, and elsewhere. The whole-bunch fermentation, minimal sulfur, and 18th-century cellar practice place De Fermo squarely in the natural-wine philosophy, while the diversified farm and biodynamic calendar reflect commitment to integrated, regenerative agriculture.
