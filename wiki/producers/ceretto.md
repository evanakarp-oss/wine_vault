## Notes

Ceretto is one of Piedmont's largest and most technically sophisticated producer-merchants, founded in 1930 by the Ceretto brothers and now a family-run powerhouse. Unlike small growers, Ceretto controls ~100 hectares of estate vineyards across Barolo and Barbaresco, plus a substantial négociant portfolio sourcing from external growers. This scale allows Ceretto to produce significant volumes while maintaining quality discipline — they are perhaps the best example of "big doesn't mean bad" in the Piedmont. The brand is synonymous with early-drinking, fruit-forward Barolo that doesn't demand 30 years in the cellar, though the best vintages age beautifully.

**Farming & Terroir Philosophy.** Ceretto converted to organic certification in recent years, and the vineyards are farmed with minimal chemical inputs. The estate's holdings span the top zones: Bricco Rocche (in Castiglione Falletto, a power site), Prapo (in Serralunga d'Alba, more earthy), Brunate (shared cru with multiple growers, limestone-rich), and significant holdings in Barbaresco (Bernadot, Bricco Asili). The soils run the gamut from clay-rich (power) to limestone-dominated (elegance). Ceretto's strategy is not to homogenize but to showcase the vineyard hierarchy: small holdings are bottled as single-vineyard releases, while broader-based fruit feeds négociant blends and Langhe cuvées.

**Winemaking Style.** Ceretto practices what might be called "classic modernism" — respecting Barolo's traditional framework (long élevage, Nebbiolo's structure) while applying modern fruit handling and oak sophistication. Fermentation is temperature-controlled, maceration is 10–14 days (shorter than traditional, favoring aromatic extraction over extraction), and oak élevage is 24 months in a mix of new (40–50%) and used Slavonian barrels. The result is wines that show Nebbiolo's character but with bright fruit, silky tannins, and an approachable mid-palate arc. Alcohol is typically 13.5–14%. The house also produces Arneis (a local white variety) and various Barbera cuvées.

**Key Vineyard Holdings & Cuvées.**
- **Barolo Bricco Rocche (Castiglione Falletto):** The flagship red. From a south-facing hill site, Bricco Rocche is broad-shouldered and fruit-forward for a Barolo, with notes of dark cherry, licorice, leather, and tar. The wine ages 10–20 years but is drinkable at 5–7 years. The 1997 (WA 96) is an iconic vintage, still drinking beautifully.
- **Barolo Prapo (Serralunga d'Alba):** From the muscular Serralunga terroir, Prapo is earthier and more structured than Bricco Rocche, with white pepper, dried herbs, and forest-floor notes. Peak at 15–25 years.
- **Barbaresco Bernadot & Bricco Asili:** Both premiers crus, perfumed with red cherry and violet, lighter-framed than the Barolos.
- **Arneis Langhe Blangé:** The entry-level white, a crisp, herbaceous counterpoint to the reds.

**House Style & Philosophy.** Ceretto is deliberately not "traditional" in the old-guard sense (think Roagna or Conterno). They embrace modern fruit expression, shorter élevage than their grandfathers would have done, and oak influence that's noticeable but not overwhelming. The house argument is that Barolo's great terroirs can sustain both interpretations — the traditional minimal-intervention route and the fruit-forward, oak-seasoned route. Ceretto chose the latter and executed it with discipline. The wines are approachable young (drinkable at 5 years) but age gracefully to 20–30 years in top vintages.

**Reputation & Critical Standing.** Ceretto has been championed by Chambers Street for 15+ years (9 articles, 2 dedicated profiles), particularly for their Bricco Rocche line. The house appears frequently in auction catalogs, a sign of secondary-market demand and collector respect. However, Ceretto is not a cult producer and doesn't command cult pricing — a bottle of 2015 Bricco Rocche might fetch $80–120 on the retail market, vs. $200+ for a boutique grower's equivalent. This positions Ceretto as a "value" producer in the sense of Piedmont quality-per-dollar, not a bargain.

**Aging Potential.** The Bricco Rocche consistently ages to 20–25 years, with the 1997 and 2000 vintages proving benchmarks. The 2010s (2010, 2011, 2013, 2015) are at their prime now (2026) and will age smoothly through the 2030s. The 2018 and 2019 vintages are mid-stream, with peak drinking 2028–2035. The house's house style (fruit-forward, moderate oak) means the wines don't require the deep cellar discipline of old-guard producers; they reward 10–20 years but are enjoyable at 5–8.

**Why Evan's Taste.** Ceretto is a pragmatic fit: it's a quality Piedmont house with serious terroir focus (not a generic producer), it maintains organic farming, and it produces wines that are architectural (structured, age-worthy) without being austere. The price-to-quality ratio is favorable compared to cult growers. Ceretto is not top-tier allocation-only material, but it's a reliable, sophisticated source for Barolo that will drink beautifully for decades. If seeking Barolo for the cellar without gambling on tiny-producer allocation luck, Ceretto is the canonical recommendation.
