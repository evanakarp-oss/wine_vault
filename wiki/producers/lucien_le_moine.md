## Notes

Lucien Le Moine operates at the opposite end of the Burgundy spectrum from mega-négociants like Jadot or Chapoutier. Rather than buying large volumes, Le Moine cherry-picks tiny parcel allocations from growers across the Côte de Nuits — often single vineyard lots of 50–200 cases — then ages them meticulously before release. The house was founded in 2003 by Lucien Le Moine (formerly of Domaine Leflaive) and quickly earned a reputation as a precision instrument: each wine is a deliberate statement about a single terroir, bottled in small numbers and released only when the winemaker judges it ready.

**Philosophy & Sourcing.** The négociant model here is one of curation, not volume. Le Moine identifies top growers and top vineyard parcels, negotiates to purchase the raw wine or must, then takes full control of élevage (aging). The sourcing criterion is simple: Côte de Nuits gravitas sites only (Gevrey grands crus, Vosne premiers crus, Morey-Saint-Denis top parcels, Chambolle-Musigny's most elegant vineyards). Each vintage, Le Moine assembles a portfolio of 12–20 different cuvées, ranging from 50 to 200 cases per wine. The result is a luxury portfolio more akin to a collector's cabinet than a production house.

**Winemaking & Oak.** Lucien Le Moine's signature is a balance between respecting the raw fruit and applying Old Burgundy wisdom. Whole-cluster fermentation is typical (60–80%), with 14–18 day macerations and natural ferments. The key decision is oak: Le Moine favors 30–50% new oak, often from Tonnellerie Rousseau or Joseph Drouhin, reasoning that judicious oak adds structure and complexity without masking the site. Élevage is 15–18 months, with one racking and minimal intervention. Alcohol sits in the 12.5–13.2% range.

**The Terroir Hierarchy.** A typical Le Moine release might include:
- A **Gevrey village** wine (entry point; silky, approachable)
- Several **Gevrey premier cru** parcels (Estournelles, Champeaux, Lavaux Saint-Jacques)
- A **Gevrey grand cru** (Chambertin or Clos Saint-Jacques single-vineyard bottling; only in top vintages)
- **Vosne-Romanée** cuvées (1er cru or village; mineral, structured)
- **Chambolle-Musigny** sites (perfumed, ethereal; often 1er cru Les Charmes or Aux Beaux Bruns)
- A **Morey-Saint-Denis** cuvée (Clos de Tart or Les Monts Luisants parcels; savory, complex)

Each is a pure expression of vineyard and vintage, with Le Moine the orchestrator rather than the winemaker.

**Reputation & Allocations.** Lucien Le Moine is the insider's négociant — not advertised on restaurant lists or retail shelves, but highly sought by Burgundy cognoscenti. The wines fetch Côte d'Or prices (Gevrey 1er cru: $60–100; Gevrey GC: $150–250; Vosne 1er cru: $80–120), justified by the parcel quality and bottling discipline. Production is aggressively limited; allocation is by relationship. The house has never chased volume and has no interest in doing so.

**Aging Potential.** Lucien Le Moine's bottles are built for the cellar. Even village-level wines peak at 8–12 years, developing earthy complexity and secondary aromas. The premier crus are sublime from year 10 onward, reaching maturity at 15–25 years depending on vintage. The grand crus (when released) are 20–30 year propositions, developing the full taxonomy of mature Pinot: leather, truffle, graphite, dried cherry, and silky tannins. The 2015s, 2016s, and 2017s are drinking beautifully now; the 2018s will peak 2028–2032.

**Why Evan's Taste.** Lucien Le Moine embodies Evan's Burgundy filter: terroir-driven, grower-quality fruit at négociant scale, precision winemaking, and elegance over opulence. The house is the rare négociant that doesn't dilute quality for volume — indeed, the business model inverts that: fewer, better bottles, priced accordingly. For collectors seeking benchmark Côte de Nuits Pinot Noir without the allocation headaches of Domaine DRC or boutique growers, Lucien Le Moine is the canonical recommendation. The wines age with the best, express site and vintage with clarity, and reward patience with complexity.
