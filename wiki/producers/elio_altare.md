## Notes

Elio Altare is a legendary Barolo innovator, credited alongside Domenico Clerico with revolutionizing Barolo's winemaking in the 1980s–1990s. Based in La Morra and farming roughly 10 hectares, Altare pioneered the use of French oak in Barolo (a heresy in traditional circles), trained in Burgundy, and returned to La Morra determined to apply those techniques to Nebbiolo. His influence on contemporary Barolo is immense — even producers who rejected his specific approach acknowledge the conversation he sparked. Altare's wines are opulent, silky, and alcohol-forward (often 13.8–14.2%) compared to traditional Barolo, and they gained cult status in the US during the 1990s–2000s.

**Philosophy & Vineyard Work.** Altare practices sustainable viticulture with emphasis on low-intervention fruit handling. His La Morra parcels are well-regarded but not the most prestigious crus — the "magic" is in the winemaking, not the terroir hierarchy. Altare's philosophy is that good fruit can be made great through technique; conversely, great terroir can be muddled by poor technique. He focuses on meticulous canopy management, careful harvest timing (ripe fruit is his signature), and low-sulfur winemaking. The result is wines that punch above their terroir weight.

**Winemaking Style.** Altare pioneered "new oak Barolo" in the 1980s, using 70–100% new French oak in élevage, a radical departure from Piedmont tradition. Modern Altare uses slightly less new oak (40–60%) but still considerable French oak influence. He also extended maceration (18–20 days, long for modernism) to extract phenolic ripeness. Fermentation is temperature-controlled, often with selected yeasts. The result is wines that are opulent, silky, and deeply fruited — dark cherry, plum, chocolate, leather — with alcohol visible in the mid-palate. These are hedonistic Barolos, not architectural ones.

**Key Cuvées.**
- **Barolo Arborina:** The main bottling, from a La Morra vineyard. Opulent, silky, with dark cherry and plum notes; drinks beautifully at 5–8 years but ages 15–25 years. The 2009 vintage (in DTE portfolio) is a benchmark modern Barolo from a top vintage.
- **Barolo La Morra:** A village-level expression, more approachable; 8–12 year aging.
- **Other parcels:** Altare also makes Barbera, Dolcetto, and various blends; all follow the fruit-forward, oak-influenced philosophy.

**Reputation & Critical Status.** Elio Altare is a legend in Barolo circles and achieved cult status in the US during the 1990s. The critical scores are strong (Parker scores in the 90s are typical for top vintages). However, allocation is complex — Altare is widely distributed through multiple channels (not allocation-only like boutique growers), but the best bottles move quickly. The house is available but not rare; pricing is moderate-to-premium (Arborina: $60–120).

**Aging Potential.** Altare's opulent style is approachable young (drinkable at 4–6 years) but ages with grace to 15–25 years in top vintages. The 2009 Arborina is drinking beautifully now (2026) and will continue to evolve through the 2030s. The 2004 and 2006 vintages are at their peaks. The house's style (ripe fruit, new oak) suggests earlier maturity but solid longevity — not the 30+ year aging curve of old-guard producers.

**Why Evan's Taste.** Elio Altare is orthogonal to Evan's stated Barolo preference. The curation note flags him as a modernist/opulent producer, and the tasting note ("incredible but out of range") positions him as admired but not on the active-buy list. Altare's fruit-forward, oak-influenced, high-alcohol style doesn't align with "WK-style undercovered/value with great farming." However, he's referenced as a canonical example of how Barolo modernism can work at high quality, and a mature bottle of 2009 Arborina is unquestionably beautiful. The house is a museum reference rather than an allocation target.
