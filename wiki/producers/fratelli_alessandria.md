## Notes

Fratelli Alessandria is one of Barolo's historic houses, tracing its roots to 1830 under the original name Fratelli Dabbene. The estate is now run by third-generation Vittore Alessandria, who has maintained the family's commitment to traditional winemaking while earning recognition as a reference producer for Verduno, one of Barolo's lesser-known but high-quality communes. The property controls roughly 12 hectares across Verduno and surrounding zones, focusing on Barolo but also producing Barbera d'Alba and Dolcetto. The winemaking style is old-school elegant — long élevage in large barrels, minimal new oak, and wines that develop secondary complexity with age.

**Vineyard Holdings & Terroir Philosophy.** Fratelli Alessandria's core holdings are in Verduno, a small commune known for producing Barolos that are lighter, more perfumed, and earlier-maturing than the heavier zones (Serralunga, Monforte). The terroir of Verduno is limestone-rich, with good drainage and elevation (300–400 meters). Alessandria also holds parcels in the Rocche dell'Annuziata and Massara crus, which command the high quality bar. The farming is traditional (not certified organic, but low-chemical input) with hand harvesting and careful fruit selection.

**Winemaking Style.** Fratelli Alessandria employs whole-cluster fermentation in large open vats, with spontaneous fermentation and 15–20 day macerations. The wine is aged 24–36 months in large Slavonian barrels (very little new oak) and is neither filtered nor fined. The result is wines of remarkable clarity and perfume: silky tannins, moderate alcohol (12.8–13.2%), and a profile dominated by red cherry, violet, dried rose, and mineral notes. The house style eschews fruit opulence in favor of structure and elegance — these are Barolos built for contemplation, not for fruit-bomb hedonism.

**Key Cuvées.**
- **Barolo Verduno:** The house's main bottling, a blend or selection from Verduno parcels. Elegant, perfumed, with silky tannins and a 12–20 year aging curve. This is the benchmarkVerduno expression.
- **Barolo Rocche dell'Annuziata:** From a more prestigious cru, slightly more structured and complex; 15–25 year aging.
- **Barolo Massara:** A secondary cru bottling, intermediate in power and elegance.
- **Barbera d'Alba:** A good secondary red, structured yet approachable.

**Reputation & Critical Standing.** Fratelli Alessandria has been championed by Chambers Street (2 dedicated articles in 2024–2026, positioning it as a "benchmark producer of Verduno"). The house is listed on Roscioli, indicating serious European recognition. However, allocation is tighter in the US than with larger producers — the house relies on direct sales and a small network of importers (Bowler Ewing) to reach North America. The CSW coverage (aging score: 2, cellar pick: true) suggests the house is valued as a long-term cellaring prospect.

**Aging Potential.** Fratelli Alessandria's wines age with grace. The Verduno bottling peaks at 12–20 years, developing silky mid-palate and complex secondary aromas (leather, tar, dried herb). The Rocche dell'Annuziata can age 20–30 years. The 2020 vintage (recently released) will drink beautifully from 2027 onward; the 2019, 2018, and older vintages are at their sweet spot now (2026).

**Why Evan's Taste.** Fratelli Alessandria is a canonical old-guard Barolo producer: traditional winemaking, no new oak, long barrel aging, wines built for patience and complexity. The house represents the "WK-style undercovered/value with great farming" axis perfectly — Verduno is less famous than Serralunga or La Morra, so pricing is favorable, but the quality is serious. If seeking aged Barolo from a historic producer with allocation availability, Fratelli Alessandria is a top recommendation.
