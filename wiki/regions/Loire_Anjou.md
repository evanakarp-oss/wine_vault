# Anjou

Warmer summers and better viticulture have added good dry whites and fragrant reds to the great sweet whites and ordinary rosés for which Anjou was best known. The schist and slate of Brittany's Armorican Massif meets the clay and limestone of the Paris Basin just south of Angers, creating slopes usefully exposed south/southwest to the sun and drying morning mist from the River Layon — a setup that, in the right years, yields sweet whites of thrilling ripeness and near-perfect acid balance.

In the southeast of the area mapped here, **Coteaux du Layon** is the broad appellation, embracing **Quarts de Chaume** — the Loire's first official Grand Cru (a mere 72 acres / 29ha, ~20 producers) — and **Bonnezeaux**, large enough (~2.5× Quarts de Chaume) to have its own AOC. The elusive **River Aubance**, parallel with the Layon to the south, also produces great sweet whites when nature cooperates. Basic **Anjou Blanc** has been in a state of benevolent transformation: selective hand-picking and sensitive use of oak are raising the standard, and dry Anjou Blanc can now be truly fine — made every year, unlike the great sweet wines. **Rosé d'Anjou** is fresher than its mawkish past; surpassed for seriousness by dry **Rosé de Loire** and off-dry pink **Cabernet d'Anjou**.

To the west of the mapped area: **Savennières** — bone-dry Chenin from steep schist slopes including the monopole **Coulée de Serrant** (Nicolas Joly) and **Roche-aux-Moines** sub-appellation.

## Geography

- **Schist + slate (Armorican)** west of Angers (Savennières, Layon)
- **Tuffeau / clay-limestone (Paris Basin)** east toward Saumur
- The Layon runs SE → NW into the Loire; morning mist from the river fuels botrytis on the right days
- ~20m contour intervals on the map; relatively gentle terrain compared to upper-Loire cliffs
