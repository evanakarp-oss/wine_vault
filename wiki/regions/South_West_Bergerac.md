# Bergerac, Monbazillac & the SW France satellites of Bordeaux

The Bergerac vineyards run east from the Bordeaux border along the Dordogne, planted to broadly the same varieties — the "jealous neighbour" zone, traditionally excluded from selling its wines under the Bordeaux name. Bergerac the basic AOC produces red, white, and rosé at all sweetness levels; **Côtes de Bergerac** is a step up, where a cut above Bergerac. Whites include the noble-rot zone of **Monbazillac**, lying just east of where the tributary Gardonette joins the Dordogne — terrain hillier than Sauternes, with Muscadelle playing a major role here rather than the minor one it plays in Sauternes. The total output of Bergerac's most famous sweet wine, Monbazillac, is 30 times greater than average quality but has improved considerably since 1993, when machine-picking was abandoned in favour of several selective harvests by hand. Much lighter doses of sulphur dioxide have become the norm, and chaptalization is banned. The best young Monbazillacs, such as those of **Château Tirecul La Gravière**, are more succulent, more sprightly than the best young Sauternes, although they soften on a distinctive amber nuttiness with age. The five-member co-op **Cave de Monbazillac** is the main producer.

**Côtes de Duras** sits between Entre-Deux-Mers (~85km away) and Bergerac — variable, but its strong point is dry Sauvignon Blanc. **Pécharmant** is distinguished by the iron in the soil, valued for its full-bodied, ageworthy reds (often used as a Médoc substitute). **Saussignac**, home to some extraordinarily distinguished producers, has only a few thousand cases. The Bordeaux right-bank-hinterland feel — the bastide of Bergerac, leading back into the stony valleys cut into the limestone — has long been a focus.

The map area also tees up two adjacent topics: **Cahors** (more famous for the hue and kingliness of its wines in the Middle Ages than today, on a grape known here as Côt, in Argentina and Bordeaux as Malbec); and the broader Lot/Lot-et-Garonne family.

## Geography

- **Substrate** — limestone (the Bordeaux right-bank family), with iron-rich soils giving Pécharmant its full-bodied character
- **Sub-zones (mapped)** — Bergerac, Montravel, Haut-Montravel, Côtes de Montravel, Rosette, Pécharmant, Saussignac, Monbazillac, Côtes de Duras
- **Climate** — slightly more continental than Bordeaux but the same Atlantic influence; the Dordogne moderates
