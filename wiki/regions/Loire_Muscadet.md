# Muscadet (Sèvre et Maine)

The Atlantic mouth of the Loire. Single grape — **Melon de Bourgogne** — over ~20,250 acres (8,200ha as of 2017), with Sèvre et Maine the best-known sub-zone (~77% of plantings). The vineyards sit on low hills of gneiss, granite, and schist; the dinner's catch is the litmus test. The river is France's longest at ~629 miles. The Atlas treats the sub-region as a family resemblance — variations of climate, soil, and tradition, with white grape varieties (Folle Blanche / Gros Plant) gradually giving way in Muscadet's favour.

The most serious Muscadet Coteaux de la Loire is made well inland around Ancenis on steep slopes of schist or granite. Sèvre et Maine, mapped here in detail, sits opposite — densely planted on low hills of various soils, mostly gneiss. Heart of the district lies around Vallet, St-Fiacre, and La Chapelle-Heulin. Crus communaux now exist (Le Pallet, Clisson, Gorges, Goulaine, Monnières-St-Fiacre, etc.) — long-élevage, lieu-dit Muscadet that has rebuilt the appellation's reputation.

## Geography

- **Sèvre et Maine** — bounded by the Sèvre Nantaise (east) and Petite Maine rivers; densely planted gneiss/granite/schist hills SE of Nantes.
- **Coteaux de la Loire** — inland around Ancenis; steeper, more severe.
- **Côtes de Grandlieu** — south of the lake, sandier.
- Climate is maritime — spring frost and autumn rain are the chief hazards (cited by the Atlas).
