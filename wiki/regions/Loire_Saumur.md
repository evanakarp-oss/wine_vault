# Saumur

The east end of the Anjou-Saumur axis, on tuffeau limestone — the same soft chalk-like rock that pocks the riverbank with cellars deep enough (some as much as ~120m / 394ft, per Ackerman) to provide constant cool maturation. **Saumur-Champigny** is the marquee red appellation, Cabernet Franc on tuffeau just SE of the town; **Saumur Puy-Notre-Dame** (~30km SW of Saumur) is a relatively new sub-appellation for fragrant, mainly Cabernet Franc reds from a wide swathe of Saumur rather than anywhere as specific as the village of Le Puy-Notre-Dame. As elsewhere in the Loire, reds have been getting stronger and darker thanks to improved vine-growing and perceptible climate change.

The cult, unsurprisingly in these parts, involves biodynamism. **Clos Rougeard** — the Foucault family domaine, after a generation in Foucault hands — was sold to the billionaire Bouygues brothers (owners of Château Montrose in St-Estèphe and a great deal besides). Almost two of every three bottles labelled Saumur today contain traditional-method sparkling wine: demand, higher permitted yields, lower risk for growers, and an obvious home for underripe grapes in difficult years. Quality varies, but the best sparkling Saumurs tend to be gently *pétillant* rather than aggressively fizzing. **Saumur Blanc**, dry Chenin from tuffeau, can be excellent too.

## Geography

- **Tuffeau** — soft cretaceous limestone, the defining substrate; gives Cabernet Franc its leafy savoury edge here
- Vineyards crowd the south bank of the Loire and along the Thouet tributary
- Saumur-Champigny boundary roughly Varrains–Chacé–Chaintres–St-Cyr-en-Bourg
- Saumur Puy-Notre-Dame sits well south of the river
