# Vouvray and Montlouis

Everything royal and romantic about France is summed up in the middle stretch of the immense river centred on Tours — a land of Renaissance châteaux, ancient towns, and beguiling white wines. Its most distinctive whites, which come in all sweetness levels and are blessed with extraordinary longevity, are made from **Chenin Blanc** grown on the low hills of soft **tuffeau** that flank the Loire. For centuries the tuffeau has provided both cellars and strange cave-dwellings — troglodytes in the literal sense.

**Vouvray** can be dry (*sec*), off-dry (*sec-tendre*, an unofficial but increasingly popular style), medium-dry (*demi-sec*), sweet (*moelleux*), or sometimes a gloriously rich expression of noble rot (*liquoreux*), requiring several different pickings (*tries*) through each vineyard. Serious producers often pick their sec and demi-sec selectively, too. It is common practice now to label still wines by site — especially the top ones. The best sites in Vouvray are on top of the cliffs overlooking the river, where a thin layer of clay and sometimes gravel overlays the limestone. The best-known producer, **Huet**, owns two: **Le Mont**, whose wines are the most concentrated, and **Clos du Bourg**, the first to be converted to biodynamic viticulture in the late 1980s. A third vineyard, **Le Haut-Lieu**, is further from the river with a much deeper layer of clay.

**Montlouis-sur-Loire** sits across the Loire — opposite Vouvray geographically, often differentiated stylistically too: Montlouis lacks the perfect sheltered, south-facing situation of the first rank of Vouvray's vineyards along the Loire, but can produce wines with just a little more tension. Vineyards close to the river have clay-limestone soils that become increasingly sandy southwards to the Cher Valley. Although still wines constitute the real glory, almost **two in every three bottles** these days contain traditional-method sparkling — demand, higher permitted yield, less risk in difficult years, and a home for underripe grapes.

## Geography

- **Tuffeau** — soft Cretaceous limestone, the defining substrate; cellars and lieux-dits carved into it
- Vouvray sits north of the Loire on south-facing slopes; Montlouis sits south of the Loire between the Loire and the Cher
- Vouvray top sites: thin clay + gravel over limestone, on cliff tops
- Montlouis: clay-limestone near the river, sandier toward the Cher
