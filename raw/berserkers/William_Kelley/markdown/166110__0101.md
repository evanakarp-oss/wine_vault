This post, and others, raise some very interesting questions—and tangents—for discussion. In fact, most of the below is a tangent about the 2019 vintage in the middle of a thread on the 2018 vintage, but here goes. For everyone’s reference, here are the rainfall statistics in 2019 by commune, as collated by the Chambre d’Agriculture of the Côte d’Or:

[2019 Côte d’Or Rainfall by Commune](https://flic.kr/p/2keGq2w) by [WilliamGFKelley](https://www.flickr.com/photos/55246154@N07/) , on Flickr

While interesting, to my mind, this data is of limited utility for understanding the 2019 vintage. For a start, the distribution is actually rather small. But, more importantly, it tells you nothing about how the vines experienced these conditions: for example, I would rather have had old vines on drought resistant rootstocks in deep soils with a favorable hydrological profile in dry Marsannay than young vines on drought-susceptible rootstocks in thin soils in (comparatively) wet Morey. Viticulture and soil management play a huge role in modulating vines’ susceptibility to drought. If one does not know the parcel-by-parcel conditions and viticultural approaches, one cannot begin to speculate about the impact of even comparatively significant disparities in rainfall (which most of these commune-by-commune differences in 2019 are not: 8% between Monthélie and Chassagne, for example). And then, in terms of determining the actual style of the wine in the glass, in a vintage such as 2019 picking date is decisive: Ghislaine Barthod, one of the earliest to pick in Chambolle-Musigny, and François Bertheau, one of the latest, will have both had the same rainfall in their parcel of Chambolle Charmes; yet the resulting wines are very different. So to cite this sort of data as if it were a critical heuristic for understanding the vintage is mistaken, I think, and also risks taking us back to the sort of reductionist vintage generalizations of yesteryear that proclaimed that such-and-such a vintage was “a great vintage for Gevrey” or whichever commune. The reality is that quality is determined in the vines on a parcel-by-parcel level, and in the cellars on a producer-by-producer level. And there is no one metric that provides the key for making sense of complex, holistic systems determined by a multitude of interrelated variables. Now, this is not intended as a rebuke to Jasper, and we all have different ways of making sense of the region and the data. But I would say that, in the glass, just to take two examples, I didn’t find Cyril Audoin’s 2019 Marsannays any less fresh and dynamic than Jeremy Seysses’ wines at Domaine Dujac in Morey–irrespective of that fact that those two communes site at opposite ends of the chart above.

The interesting tangent here, to my mind, is the broader question about the place for data in wine criticism. I see more and more reviews, especially of Bordeaux wines, that give pHs, IPTs and other numbers. These are indeed objective “facts” about the wines, but what information can actually be derived from them? I tend not to record this sort of information, unless a wine is obviously defined in the glass by for example a very high or very low pH, or a very high level of alcohol—and the numbers confirm this.

I also can’t resist getting into the question of paradigm shifts. If anyone wanted to talk about drawing lines in 1989 and 2003, they would have my endorsement, but it seems to me a little premature to draw a line after 2017. 2003, from before the division, remains more extreme in terms of temperature extremes, accumulation, and number of consecutive days >100 F than 2020, 2019 or 2018. 2015, 2009 and (arguably) 2005 would also sit quite easily, to my mind, in the company of 2020, 2019 and 2018 in terms of conditions and, making allowances for changing winemaking approaches, wine styles. To situate things in a deeper context, back in the 18th century, Dom. Dénise wrote that the best wines in Burgundy were generally made in vintages with mid-September harvests: well, that was the case in 2019! So let’s see what the next few years bring, and let’s not give up on Burgundy just yet.

The remark that producers have also learnt to make fresher wines in extreme vintages is also very true. I have hesitated to get into this subject, as I do not want to be type-cast as an “apologist” for super-ripe vintages: for the record, many of the most profound Burgundies I have drunk were wines made from what would today be considered marginally ripe fruit, and the aromatic range of cool, late vintages is very hard to march. I have more 2016s and 2017s in my cellar than 2015s and 2018s. But, it clearly is possible to make much “fresher” wines in warm vintages by picking early in the morning, using refrigerated trucks to keep fruit cool, adapting vinification and élevage practices. 2003s were mostly vinified like 2002s, 2001s, 2000s etc before them, and that often didn’t work. 2020s, by contrast, were made with the benefit of accumulated experience of a handful of very warm vintages, and it really does show. So be cautious about over-extrapolating.

Adaptation in the vines and wineries is clearly possible: Bertrand Dugat at Domaine Claude Dugat barely exceeded 13% in 2018, 2019 and 2020—and his wines in these years are beautifully refined, perfumed and pure (FYI, several of his 2017s were a hair below 12%). His vineyard work is impeccable and he timed his harvest carefully. Because some producers failed to adapt and made 16% monsters, or perhaps even wanted to make 16% monsters, do not infer that this was unavoidable or that it needs to be the new normal. Picking over-ripe fruit is a choice, even if it’s harder to dial in on the perfect date in a warm, sunny year when everything moves very fast.
