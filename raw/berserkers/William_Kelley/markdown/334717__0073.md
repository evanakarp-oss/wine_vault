I think there are a lot of variables…

What we are talking about here is the *perception* of phenolic “grip”, in other words, a sort of bitterness. For the perception of bitterness:

- quality is more important than quantity: unripe phenolics, even if less abundant, are perceptibly bitterer
- acidity amplifies the perception of bitterness
- residual CO2 amplifies the perception of bitterness
- high levels of free SO2 amplify the perception of bitterness

My perception, as far from an expert on the region, is that these four criteria are all often met by Hunter Valley Sémillon. Whether the analytically measurable levels of dry extract are all that high, however, is another matter.

White Burgundy made from concentrated fruit, crushed heavily and pressed firmly can easily have >20g/l dry extract (a crude proxy for what we are talking about, but at least a quantifiable one). Due to ripeness and barrel maturation on the lees, however, they need not taste “coarse” or crudely bitter/astringent. Rather, the bitterness is integrated into a harmonious whole and passes unperceived by many tasters.
