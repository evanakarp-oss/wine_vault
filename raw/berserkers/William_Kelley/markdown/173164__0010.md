This is a region that’s very close to my heart, and which I’ve made a point to cover annually in TWA, along with all the “lesser known” AOCs of Burgundy, most of which suffer from the same vicious cycle of negative consumer perceptions that impose low prices, encouraging producers to privilege quantity and cash flow over quality, resulting in mediocre wines that reinforce those negative perceptions (I am looking at a book from the 19th century that laments Mercurey’s lost reputation, describing precisely this vicious cycle). There are more and more producers making very decent wine for a good price in the Côte Chalonnaise, but what excites me more are the handful that are going even further, trying to break that vicious cycle and make wines that prove that the limitations under which “lesser” AOCs operate are as much imposed as revealed. I would put Dureuil-Janthial and Bruno Lorenzon at the top of that list, as anyone who follows my work will know, but others e.g. François Lumpp (one of the first to plant high quality massale selections taken from the Côte d’Or in the Côte Chalonnaise) have been important too.

One producer folks might not have heard of is Alain Hasard of Domaine Les Champs de l’Abbaye. He does everything right (massale selections, high density, organic, hand harvesting at maturity, classical winemaking with well-chosen barrels and long élevage: the things that make great e.g Givry just as surely as they make great Gevrey!) and his wines are very sensual and charming—and age well, too. He’s the only estate-bottler in the village of Aluze, which has some top sites that are “only” entitled to Bourgogne Côte Chalonnaise AOC, but which are much more interesting from an agronomic perspective than most Bourgogne on the plain in front of the Côte d’Or (sloping clay-limestone soils with prime exposure and drainage, rather than flat, endless mud). One could make the case that Aluze merits an AOC of its own just as much as Bouzeron. Anyway, the wines were a revelation to me and I highly recommend them.

Looking at the younger generation, there is plenty of promise in the region, but two new arrivals who are definitely following through so far are Gautier Desvignes at Domaine Desvignes in Givry and Maxime Cottenceau in Buxy (a Dureuil alumnus, most of his vineyards are in Montagny).

The Côte Chalonnaise is also a very beautiful place to spend time, with a couple of decent rustic restaurants, so it’s definitely worth a day trip if anyone is ever in Burgundy. It is quite easy to get a handle, at least superficially, on the Côte d’Or (south-east facing limestone escarpment, check). But the Côte Chalonnaise is quite complicated: Rully, for example, has a first hillside in front of the village; and a second overlooking the village; which in turn becomes a valley leading up to a plateau (largely hidden from view) above the village. Much generic négociant Rully comes from machine harvested fruit grown on this plateau. Meix Cadot, for example,  sits on the first and warmest slope in front of the village; and Chapître, to take another, is in the heart of the second slope overlooking the village, a real sun trap; whereas e.g. Grésigny is towards the end of the valley that winds round up to the plateau, with great exposition but a much cooler mesoclimate. Figuring out stuff like this helps make sense of why Meix Cadot has such weight; why Chapître is so fleshy and precocious; and why Grésigny is so tensile. But it is not easy to find resources that explain all of this. So visiting bears dividends.

If anyone has any questions they want to ask about the region, I’d be happy to try to field them to the best of my abilities!
