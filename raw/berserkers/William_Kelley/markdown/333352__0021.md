Following the tangent: as a winemaker during élevage, you are trying to add as little SO2 (antiseptic / antioxidant / anti-oxidase) as possible, as late as possible. The longer you can defer those additions, the more stable the wine seems to be, and the freer its expression. But, that is fraught with risks: principally from yeast (mainly brettanomyces) and bacteria. It is often rewarding to take risks, but it is important to take them intelligently: it is always better to realize 90% reward than to push for 100% and end up loosing 30%; perfect is sometimes the enemy of (very) good. Brettanomyces can be monitored regularly, and their production of phenols impeded by low cellar temperatures, so that is actually quite manageable. Bacteria, however, can be more risky, and they love the conditions that brettanomyces don’t like. So the moment you perceive anything mousey/funky/slimey in a wine, it’s important to intervene very quickly.
