Yeah, I think Mel has it the wrong way round (IIRC, Clark is wary of excessively cold cellars for this reason and points to it as a cause of high VAs in Burgundy).

The phenomenon is responsible for a quite interesting phenomenon that is observable in white wines in very cold cellars. Mine go from a high of 13C (capped there with a/c but we don’t need to run it for more than two months of the year) to a low of 5C during the winter. You get dissolved oxygen during the winter but the cellar is so cold you don’t get any oxidation. Then in early spring as the cellar warms up that plays out and the wines go through a bit of an oxidative phase. The temptations is to sulfite, but if you don’t sulfite and maybe just agitate the lees in late spring the wines come back towards reduction by the next harvest and then actually reduce in the next winter. This can be quite disconcerting!
