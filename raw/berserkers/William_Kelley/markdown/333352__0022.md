I don’t think I drew too many analogies with the contemporary visual arts, but I’m happy to pursue the comparison.

Art can be produced by two fundamentally different categories of people: artisans and artists. Winemakers are, to me, clearly artisans: this is the world of learned crafts, agglomerated incremental gains, iteration and an obsessive, creative love. Most art, for most of recorded history, was made by artisans, not “artists”. Artists, as opposed to artisans, arrived on the scene relatively recently (turn of the 20th century), and the cultural history of it would be interesting to get in to, in particular with regards to the cult of “originality” as defined as something totally new as opposed to something reinterpreted.

To you, the artists you cite are paradigm shifting; but to me, Pollock made a mess, Duchamp was a practical joker, and Warhol self-consciously trivial. More power to them for having made a lot of money. But they were not engaged in a pursuit that would have been experientially and motivationally recognizable to Botticelli or Turner. And I am just fine if wine has nothing in common with that sort of art.
