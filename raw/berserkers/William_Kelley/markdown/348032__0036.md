Those are not clones, but families of selections, much as so-called “Pinot fin” refers to a wide range of different Pinot selections that share similar morphology (small berries and bunches, open clusters) but which are not genetically identical.

“Aligoté doré” just refers to the tendency of ripe bunches of aligoté to go golden/red colored, as other varieties such as Roussanne do at maturity. And the various highly productive clones tend never to attain this degree of maturity, with the berries remaining green and unripe. Ripe Aligoté looks like this:

There are a few high quality massale selections of Aligoté, some of them proprietary, that produce small berries and high quality wines, so there are some new plantings of this material. But the majority of Aligoté matching this description along the Côte d’Or is to be found in old pre-clonal vineyards. Since even low-yielding Aligoté tends to remain productive over time, and it has historically been worth so little, re-planting has tended to be an economically marginal proposition, so there is quite a lot of old vine aligoté intact in the regional AOCs.
