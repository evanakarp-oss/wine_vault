I hope you won’t consider this a breach of the peace, Donald, and I have hitherto refrained from getting into this discussion on the grounds that, as the French say, les goûts et les couleurs ne se discutent pas, and that to each their own. But why don’t you like Ulysse Collin? And I’m talking about the wines, not the distribution system. I’m all in favor of robust exchange, and every proposition should be interrogated, but you’ve given us a lot of invective without much in the way of analysis. I could understand not liking the style, but “worthless swill”?

For my part, I love the wines for their texture and intensity of flavor; their aromas, patinated by élevage and reserve wines but without the exaggerations of oxidation or dominant oak; for their tension and precision without any excess of asperity; and for the relentless work that has gone into making them better every year for the more than a decade that I’ve been visiting and tasting. As someone who has sometimes criticized growers for disgorging too soon and shipping too soon after disgorgement, surely you can at least appreciate Olivier’s initiatives in that regard? Or his decision to constitute such large stocks of reserve wines? To me, it doesn’t really make sense to compare this unfavorably to a big house that only owns just over 30 hectares of vineyards, a fraction of its requirements; that outsources its viticulture to Veuve Cliquot; and that less than a decade ago were still using herbicides in the vineyard that produced at the time the most expensive wine in Champagne. As Michel Bettane once wrote of Egly, it would be unreasonable to expect big houses to be able to attain the same level of agronomic and technical excellence given their larger scale, and I don’t make these points even to criticize, just to say that we are comparing chalk and cheese.

Indeed, the most intelligent big houses today have understood that to compete they must become great farmers and pivot to a different approach: Roederer and Bollinger have been on this path for some time; and now DP, for example, are phasing out the luminous bottles and moving towards more and more late disgorgement, clearly reorienting their brand. As more and more small growers decide to keep their best grapes for themselves, and as a more extreme climate throws into relief differences in farming approaches, big houses dependent on purchased fruit and must are going to have a harder and harder time applying the requisite pixie dust in the cellar to maintain quality, and it is going to be very interesting to see where we end up in a decade.
