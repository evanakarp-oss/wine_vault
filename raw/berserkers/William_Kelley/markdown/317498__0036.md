The prerequisites for that are ripe grapes and winemaking/maturation/bottling practices adapted to producing long-lived wines.

In theory, these could be replicated in most places in the Beaujolais crus, especially today where you can even get good ripeness in e.g. Chiroubles; but for historical reasons the best terroirs of Moulin-a-Vent (under which label the best wines of Fleurie and Chénas were often sold) have tended to be the ones that have been handled that way by their producers. That can be a double-edged sword, as MaV is sometimes the victim of “aspirational” winemaking with clumsy extraction and in-your-face oak, something that’s more seldom inflicted on less prestigious crus.
