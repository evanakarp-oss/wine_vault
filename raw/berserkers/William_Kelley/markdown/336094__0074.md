People drank the 1929s in the 1930s, the 1953s in the 1950s… Saintsbury was drinking 1864s in the 1870s… In the 1980s, Penning Rowsell in the fifth edition of his Bordeaux book wrote about the 1961s as if they were fully mature and liable to decline. So I think this idea that you need two decades minimum to appreciate Bordeaux is rather aberrant in the longer culture of wine connoisseurship. Certainly, a lot of wines in the second half the 20th century were very austere, with unripe tannins, lacking in fruit/floral aromas due to oxidative handling and/or brettanomyces and/or low levels of anisoles in the winery. With time, those wines have mellowed to become more appealing. But to me that has more to do with production methods than the terroirs and grape varieties.
