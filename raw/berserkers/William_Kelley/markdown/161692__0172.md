> *Replying to :*  
> Without wishing to be gratuitously pedantic, the Côte de Beaune is, along with the Côte de Nuits, part of the Côte d’Or. And there are many producers in the Côte de Beaune whose red wines match the best of the Côte de Nuits in terms of depth, complexity and longevity: take Lafarge’s Volnay Clos des…

I should have been clearer: my point was that they are not second-class wines that can’t conformably sit on the same table as the best wines of the Côte de Nuits. I suspect you’d agree with that. I can think of plenty of instances where top wines of the Côte de Beaune rival or surpass top wines of the Côte de Nuits: put 1989 La Tâche up against Lafarge’s 1989 Clos des Chênes, for example, or the 1985 Pousse d’Or 60 Ouvrées against Jean Gros’ 1985 Richebourg. I remember a Leroy Corton-Bressandes 1949 dominating the Richebourg of the same year. These things happen often enough to dispel prejudices against the wines of the Côte de Beaune. And what’s more, the red wines of the Côte de Beaune today are arguably better than they have been for decades.
