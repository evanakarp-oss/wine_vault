The story is a bit more complicated than that. As is the story about who is actually the consultant enologist at this estate in reality, as opposed to on paper.

2018 and to a lesser extent 2015 are vintages that I suspect many Bordeaux purists will struggle to love, and I do not own any wines from those vintages myself, with the exception of some Lafleur. It’s a pity that 2016, 2017, 2019 or 2020 weren’t part of the vertical to get a better bearing on the true merits of the new Figeac.

Figeac is a large estate, with quite a variety of expositions, soils, and mesoclimates, as well as an unusual encépagement for Saint-Émilion; so one of the big changes has been to extend picking over a longer period, starting earlier and finishing later. Agronomic improvements have also occurred in terms of how and when soils are cultivated and the use of cover crops and canopy management to mitigate climatic extremes. And a massale selection program has been going for a decade now to preserve the best vine genetics and gradually improve (as at most estates in Bordeaux, after the 1956 frosts the overall quality of vine genetics took a hit, even if Figeac was managed very thoughtfully in that era). Then, in the cellar, brett has been eliminated and cooperage choices refined; initially the percentage of new oak did rise, and I think that resulted in some supplemental mid-palate creaminess in the 2010s, but one reason I like the 2020 so much is its integration and the fact that it is very firmly Figeac in its identity. The 2016 is its closest rival in the last decade but the élevage isn’t quite so precise.
