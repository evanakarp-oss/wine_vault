> *Replying to :*  
> Sorry to quote my own post, but I would be very interested in having any non professional wine “experts” and especially the wine critics (William Kelley and Jasper Morris, both of whom attend the Burgfest blind tasting…at least “producer blind” by vineyard) comment on whether they look for typicity…

Being premised on the concept of “typicity” (in itself, like “terroir”, a French word “typicité” that has simply been Anglicized), your question could easily become circular!

For me, the influence of site is best described as a “disposition”. Terroir is sometimes portrayed as if it were the case that each vineyard has an ideal “type” against which any given wine may be judged (hence “typicity”). Yet, as others have observed, in reality the differences between producers within a vineyard are so great as to make a mockery of such a notion: which Clos de la Roche conforms more closely to the typological ideal of Clos de la Roche—Dujac’s, Lignier’s, Ponsot’s or Leroy’s? The answer to this question is not something that can be objectively verified, it is largely a matter of taste and opinion.

To elaborate on what I mean by terroir lending wines a disposition: Cros Parantoux almost invariably has a lower pH than Richebourg, so Richebourg will almost invariably be the broader, more enveloping wine; whereas Cros Parantoux will be more tensile. Criots-Bâtard-Montrachet is a riper, more humid site than Chevalier-Montrachet, so all things being equal Criot will be the rounder, fatter and often more exotic wine. Meursault Rougeots tends to be blockier and more muscular than Meursault Chevalières, which is more chiseled. Such dispositions are most apparent when tasting in a single producer’s cellar, as that eliminates more variables (though it would be an error to assume that this eliminates all variable: differences of vine genetics, vine age, farming and winemaking can still be significant at the same address).

But even if a sense of these dispositions is part of the total context that informs how I review a wine, in my view it is not really a metric against which quality can be usefully measured. To return to a producer I’ve already cited for an example: Olivier Lamy’s Criots-Bâtard-Montrachet is more tensile and incisive than most Chevalier-Montrachet, but I am certainly not going to mark it down for that—on the contrary. The less academic qualities that make for a wine that gets a great review will be familiar: including complexity, intensity, structure, freshness and more impressionistic concepts such as elegance and harmony. I have not yet encountered wines which display an abundance of these qualities that I was inclined to fault for lack of “typicity”.
