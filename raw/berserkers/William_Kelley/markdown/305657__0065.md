Hi Howard!

It is not so much being smart, as not knowing where to start…

On the one hand, I’m obviously flattered by the compliments I’ve been paid in this thread. And I certainly wouldn’t want my reticence to be mistaken for indifference: I’m sincerely grateful to anyone who takes an interest in my work, and more than delighted if that work is enriching their wine experience.

Equally, however, I feel a little diffident. I have never sought, or had the pretension, to position myself as “the next” RP, and I cannot and do not claim any kind of apostolic succession: we have met a few times, and exchanged a few emails, but no more than that. I am also very wary of what the sociologist Dan Sperber calls the “guru effect”: I hope that I scrupulously avoid arguments from authority in my reviews, and try to give the reasoning why I esteem particular producers and wines in such a way that those with differing priorities and preferences will find the means with which to disagree with me in the reviews themselves. And while I certainly aspire to give candid reviews without fear or favor (I prefer that phrase to “objective”, but let’s not get into philosophy), I am acutely conscious, as a wine producer in my spare time, that no judgement is infallible in wine. This is why I value the Donald Pennets of this world, cantankerous and unreasonable though they may sometimes be: if wine is a matter of taste, excessive consensus in wine should make us all uneasy.

I do admire, and draw inspiration from, much of RP’s body of work. Above all, his willingness to disrupt traditional/established hierarchies and taste the wine for what it is seems more relevant than ever today, as wine writing becomes harder and harder to distinguish from advertorial, and it’s all too easy to name the winners in advance. No influential voice has an unmitigatedly positive influence, but I tend to think that we take for granted a lot of the positives of RP’s influence: who else dared to give great reviews to the wines of the Mâconnais, or a young guy named Anselme Selosse, or the supposedly rustic country wines of Cornas, in the early to mid 1980s? Those reviews in some cases changed the landscape of wine, saving vineyards that might have become vacation houses for wealthy Lyonnais. Then there’s his Stakhanovite work ethic, which I certainly can’t match.

But I also think that wine writing needs to evolve. What do I aspire to achieve? I want to bring deeper context and depth to wine reviews. At its most elementary, that simply means visiting the producers: I learnt wine criticism tasting in the Côte d’Or, where you have to visit everyone; it was only later that I discovered that most other wine regions were reviewed in massed comparative tastings. I try to review all the regions I cover the way I review the Côte d’Or: visiting the producers, understanding their practices (for better or worse). This is not about simply hearing what they have to say and repeating it; and indeed some producers in Bordeaux, where it was always an axiom that a site visit would equal a better score, have been perplexed that I am liable to visit their vineyards but also not like their wines. Instead, it’s about verifying the coherence of the discourse, the tasting, and the actuality on the ground.

I also hope to bridge what I perceive as a widening gap between producers (and by producers I mean the people who actually make the wines, so not always the proprietors) and consumers. My article on Bordeaux’s “state of the art” was an attempt to do this. Farming and making some wine of my own has given me some insight into production, and a few ways to break the ice and get people to speak candidly about what they do. The corollary is that I hope to foreground the important of great agronomy and technique (in the sense that one might compliment a musician’s technique) in wine production. A variety of reasons mean that great farming, even in the greatest appellations, is rarer than it should be; and I think wine writers should know the difference between a vineyard lavished with 1,000 hours per hectare manual work by a salaried team vs one that has seen 300 hours per hectare of subcontracted labor. I should probably go further on this subject, especially in the Côte d’Or, and it’s a project I have in mind.

I also tend to think we are moving away from an era when people sought simplification of the complex to where they seek to understand more deeply—at least in this very particular niche. I try to regularly remind myself that the raison d’être of a wine review is to explain why the wine should be of interest, and if possible to explain how it got to taste the way it tastes. I’m not trying to offer any short cuts, but rather (and I hope without being too élitist), offer insights into the complex questions. For me, the real potential “competitors” I think about are passionate (and, for my regions, wealthy) consumers who try to get to the heart of what makes a given wine interesting. I hope to be able to bring something to the table that even people who can drink these wines every night can’t get, by going deeper into the process behind them.

Anyway, these are just a couple of my thoughts about the big and complex subject that is the future of wine writing. Thanks once again to everyone who had something to say; it’s interesting/flattering/salutary to read. And if I don’t do much in the way of follow-up posting, it’s because I’m a little behind with my Champagne report!

All best,

William.
