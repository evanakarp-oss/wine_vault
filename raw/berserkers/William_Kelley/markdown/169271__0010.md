> *Replying to :*  
> Ouch, William… any exceptions to that rule of thumb?

To me, the wines took a step up with 09, 10, 12, 15… but, some of these vintages, having tasted great in barrel, developed brett in bottle.

All in all, I think that, by virtue of being a very charming guy and prices being very “inexpensive for a grand cru”, Thierry Brouin and the estate’s owners were given a pass for overcropping, planting poor quality clones/selections of Pinot Noir, formulaic vinification (huge tanks, and rotofermenters for the lower appellations), and sloppy élevage. Wines such as 1919, 1923, 1934, 1937 and 1947 Clos des Lambrays can compete with anything in Burgundy, and that should be what the estate aspires to achieve!
