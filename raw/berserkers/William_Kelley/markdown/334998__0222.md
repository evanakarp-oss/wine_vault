It’s quite complex so I’ll expand… On the one hand, less clean and less completely and homogeneously ripe fruit produces wines that are more fragile to oxidation. On the other, many châteaux also like to show samples that were drawn the day before, or two days before, because if not the samples can also reduce in the sample bottle—but obviously this can backfire, too. On top of that, 2024, perhaps due to residues of phytosanitary products from harvest or maybe just the less clean quality of fruit, could also be quite reductive. So as opposed to 2022 samples which were quite robust and could be kicked around, we encountered quite a few 2024 samples that were a bit tired, and also quite a few that were notably reduced. At least some châteaux told us they upped the SO2 levels in the samples to protect them; but that can in turn suppress fruit and foreground tannin: and we did taste some samples that seemed muted by quite high free SO2. Add to this the more delicate structural matrices of the wines, which could show their new oak quite a bit on some days, and not at all on others, and I think you get a sense of why tasting EP is quite a demanding technical exercise, irrespective of any aesthetic judgements.
