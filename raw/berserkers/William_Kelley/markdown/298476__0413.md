I certainly understand your perspective, but I don’t share it!

For me, the two are sides of the same coin: my “state of the art” article is something of a manifesto for what I consider worth championing in contemporary Bordeaux, and the long-form elaboration of the rationale behind which estates succeeded in 2020 and 2022, and which didn’t. My en primeur report is animated by the same vision of contemporary Bordeaux that is expressed in the long article.

Bordeaux is and has always been a commercially spirited wine producing region, and anyone who hopes to influence it without engaging with the region’s commercial calendar is doomed to failure. Not to mention the fact that if TWA were to unilaterally stop en primeur coverage, we would surely hemorrhage subscribers. The vacuum would simply be filled by the rest of the commentariat.

So, while I am aware, as you know, of the limitations of tasting unfinished wines, rather than abandoning EP coverage, my aspiration is simply to do it better. That means bringing the experience of following wines throughout their élevage to the task; visiting almost all of the estates reviewed in this report sur place; tasting multiple times over a long period; following the estates’ vineyards and technical practices over the course of the year; and now, tasting together with a colleague who is based in Bordeaux year round, and whose network means we can find out exactly who e.g. failed to treat on a Sunday and was thus hit hard by mildew, or who has a TCA problem in their chai, or who chaptalized and osmosed their Merlot in 2020… all of that put together leaves me happy with the work we are doing, the limitations of barrel tastings notwithstanding. And it is essential to advancing our vision for Bordeaux’s wines.
