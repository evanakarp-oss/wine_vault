I would bet money that all of those were chaptalized. I’m not going to text/email every winemaker at those estates, especially as we’re entering into a holiday week in France, but pre-2015 chaptalization was pretty ubiquitous for Cabernet in the Médoc, even in ripe years and ripe terroirs (1990 Cos d’Estournel was chaptalized 1% for example). The same was true of Burgundy, including the very best estates. Of course, ingredient labelling will bring total transparency to this and any other additions/amendments. But I would say (a) that it is the other enological manipulations that you haven’t heard of that you should be focussing on (b) this isn’t the critique I’d be making of the less successful 2024 Bordeaux. As someone who has actually chaptalized a wine made from grapes they grew, hopefully I have an informed perspective! My thoughts in the Burgundy-centric thread cited above are pretty much extensible to Bordeaux, with the added emphasis that even if it’s ripe a low pH Cabernet Sauvignon at 11.5% is a pretty unfriendly customer—and that’s just the nature of the variety in certain circumstances—and an extra degree can really make the wine taste more like itself, in the same way that a pinch of salt makes a fresh tomato taste more like itself.
