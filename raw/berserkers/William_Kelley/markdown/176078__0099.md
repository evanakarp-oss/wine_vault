> You may want to check that. My French isn’t great, but I’m pretty sure Dauvissat has been bottling and selling wine under their own label since the 1930s. When I was there, they had barrels that dated back to the 1950s. Sure, but 100% domaine bottling since 1922? I don’t think so. Even Leflaive…

Your attention to detail prompted me to look up the dates in Khiem Le’s excellent book, where he quotes René Dauvissat. They began direct sales to the final client in 1931, but the wine was still shipped in bulk, not domaine bottled:
> Wine was rarely bottled at the estate.  Instead, it was sold in cask.  “We ensured that the vinification of our wines was finished, before shipping them in >> feuillettes chablisiennes> > (132-liter barrels).  Initially, the bottling took place on the customers’ premises—in restaurants, grocery stores, and wine shops.  Subsequently, Parisian establishments had difficulty in executing this task because competent cellar hands became rare.  As a result, customers started to buy bottled wine directly from us.  And from 1950, improvements in road transportation allowed us to ship our wine in bottles, labeled as Domaine Robert Dauvissat, while continuing to send wine in feuillettes to Paris for another 15 years.”> “Several well-known French actors bought wine from us, but we had a much more illustrious customer.  A Parisian wine merchant ordered a quarteau, or 66-liter barrel, of La Forest each year.  We learned that his principal client was Monsignor Roncalli, the Apostolic Nuncio (the Vatican ambassador) to France from 1944 to 1953.  He loved Chablis and served it to his guests.  He then became Pope: John XXIII was Monseigneur Roncalli!  We have always felt sure that it was thanks to the wine of Chablis, ‘which enlightens the spirit,’ that he had the idea to launch the Second Vatican Council!”
From a winemaking perspective, this is interesting because of the use of new feuillettes as opposed to the old 228-liter barrels of today.
