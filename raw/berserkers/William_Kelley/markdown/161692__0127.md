His Criots is also high density, and it has more dimension and nuances and muscle (obviously it’s a riper site) than the Derrière Chez Edouard HD - but there aren’t the sort of flagrant differences in concentration, tension, and persistence that one often finds at other addresses as you ascend the appellation hierarchy. In that respect, tasting Lamy’s HD wines—the Tremblots, the Derrière Chez Edouard and the Criots—is similar to tasting, for example, Lalou’s Chambolle wines at Domaine Leroy (as I adverted to before). What is going on is that the farming and winemaking at that level emphasize differences in character while significantly effacing the obvious differences in quality (concentration, phenolic maturity, persistence) that often separate communal and grand cru bottlings: made that way, the Criots and the Musigny are better not because they have more concentration or structure but because they have more nuance and more layers. My point is that it’s harder to push these lower appellations into that sort of territory which the grands crus more readily inhabit, but that it’s clearly possible—and I hope we will see more people attempting it in the years to come. The shrugging “it’s only a village wine, all the same” attitude is surprisingly pervasive to this day, but the economic reality has changed and there is really no need for any domaine in the heart of the Côte d’Or to be farming any of their AOCs for volume these days.
