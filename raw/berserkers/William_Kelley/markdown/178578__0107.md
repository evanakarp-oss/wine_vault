I am indeed making a concerted effort to restore some sort of sanity and coherence to Bordeaux scoring. To me, if scores are to have any value, they must permit the reviewer to make fine distinctions between the profound, the great, and the good; and in the 2020s, saying that the wines of today all deserve immensely high scores because they are better than the wines of the 1970s is beginning to become a bit of a straw man. It is a difficult escalator to exit, however, and only a few weeks ago, for example, the proprietor of a very good château, whose 2019 I had scored over 96/100, began our conversation with the words “So you didn’t like my 2019?”. In Burgundy, no one has ever complained to me about receiving a 96/100 (or any other score, for that matter). The commercial director of another estate, who I scored less well, but still to my mind quite kindly, tried to tell me that the score I had given their wine “wasn’t in keeping with the dignity of the château”, and insisted, laughably, that his wine was as good as the 2019 Cheval Blanc that I scored 100/100. However, I am determined to persist with this endeavor, because it is in fact in everyone’s interests that scores retain credibility, and while getting very high scores is obviously gratifying to proprietors’ egos, I do think consumers are developing “score fatigue”, and that a little more continence in giving out the high numbers will be followed and ultimately respected by the market.
