While there are certainly plenty of lovely 2014s, and it is a vintage I purchased for my own cellar, re-tasting them extensively from bottle (250 or so this year) has been a little surprising, as the succulent fruit and classical profile that made them so appealing from barrel is, in some cases, giving way to a tannic structure which is both more assertive and less phenolically mature than expected. Several winemaker friends have seconded this perception. And I begin to wonder whether the comparatively short 2014 ripening season (90 days vs a traditional 100) will ultimately express itself in tannins which are a bit less ripe.

By contrast, 2013s, which often seemed a bit meager and angular from barrel, are now often filling out nicely, and this is actually the vintage where I would venture that careful shoppers can find bargains. Interestingly, 2013 was a protracted season which, according to Christophe Roumier, corresponds very closely in terms of the raw data to 1978 (cool, with 110 days of hang-time). At first glance the comparison isn’t obvious, but it may well be that in the last decade we have forgotten how to taste vintages like 1978 from barrel… But in any case, I am actively looking to taste and purchase more 2013s.

As for 2015s, the serious producers really didn’t produce near-term wines, and many are already beginning to shut down. Anyone who thinks that 2015 has anything in common with 2009 and purchases them for near-term pleasure is likely to be sorely disappointed. It depends on the style of the domaine, of course, but even e.g. Mugneret-Gibourg’s Bourgogne rouge will clearly be better at age 15 than it is at age 10. Many of the higher appellation wines currently taste a bit inchoate and very primary, even if their mid-palates are sufficiently ample to conceal their prodigious tannins for the moment. If you do catch them when they’re open for inspection, of course, they will be ‘pleasure bombs’ - but I don’t think they will be delivering an appreciably different experience to a luscious 2015 cru Beaujolais, whereas the tariff will be considerably higher; you will be tasting the sunshine and the fruit, but not really what you paid for.
