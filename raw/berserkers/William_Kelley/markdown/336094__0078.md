I think one trap that people fall into when discussing the aging of wine is extrapolating from the fact that the e.g. 1949 Châteaux X is amazing today that the e.g. 2000 Châteaux X must some day become just as amazing if only you wait long enough. In reality so much has changed: the clonal and agrochemical revolution of the 1970s, yields, winemaking practices and aesthetic ambitions, the climate… (To say nothing of varietal composition or even the plots of land making up the estate itself). Waiting for the hypothetical 2000 in this thought experiment to “come around” may be akin to someone who loves espresso saying that the iced latté they have been served will be great when it eventually warms up. If you read how people described the great pre-modern (if we can call them that) Bordeaux when those wines were young, it is clear that vintages such as 1929 were not short on complexity in their youth. As for 1928s, they are in many cases still taut and angular. They are much easier to find than 1929s, however, because nobody wanted to drink them—for much the same reason that 1870 is one of the most easily found 19th-century vintages.
