They are better than in the 1990s for sure: sorting and processing have improved a lot. The best producers are less inclined to penny pinch when it comes to buying the best, too. But as it has been explained to me, there are issues with the forests growing faster in general, but droughts then slowing growth, resulting in uneven growth and corks that are more likely to have problems. Only the other day I found a 2018 Genevrières in my cellar from a (the?) top producer that was leaking **through** the cork. And I opened a 2019 Chambolle that showed exactly the same problem. These are bottles that went direct from the winery to my cellar in Beaune in November, so not a heat issue…

On top of that, there’s huge demand for the best cork, as well as growing demand for cork from DIAM and others producing composites who have a lot of clout in the market compared to some of the smaller premium cork merchants.
