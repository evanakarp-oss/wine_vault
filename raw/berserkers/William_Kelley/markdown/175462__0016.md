Answering the question in a different way, I’d have to say the shift towards longer macerations that really took off in the mid-19th century. Eighteenth-century Burgundy was generally made with 24-36-hour macerations and only “kept” for a few years. For a variety of reasons, this changed in the following century, with month-long macerations being quite typical. It’s hard to exaggerate what a fundamental aesthetic difference this must have made to wine styles. It was also debated at the time: in 1860, Guyot was deploring the trend for longer and longer macerations, arguing that two weeks was more than ample. I don’t think any of the swings of the stylistic pendulum in the 20th and 21st centuries can match the change that took place between 1790 and 1890.
