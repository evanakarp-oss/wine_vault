To add a critic’s perspective to the discussion, it is certainly a complicated issue to address. When we review the new vintage, it is impossible to predict premox. And it is very difficult to organize a tasting of the kind that Don puts together here (which is one reason why I would love to participate). I buy a lot of white Burgundy myself, but my purchases have always been made with a view to *avoiding* premox, as opposed to having an interesting representative sample set. That means I am very selective about producers and that when I buy older wines (which I do frequently), they are mostly from before 1995. This works pretty well for me: I drank a lot of white Burgundy last year, I can count the number of advanced/premoxed bottles I had last year on two hands, and only two of those bottles were from my cellar. In fact, we all have very different experiences: I drink (as opposed to “taste”) around 20 to 30 bottles of Dauvissat a year, for example, and I never had an oxidized bottles in France; whereas Herwig has had oxidized bottles in France / Belgium. Indeed, I would suggest that sommeliers at top restaurants with deep Burgundy lists likely have a much more statistically meaningful impression of the state of premox than most wine writers.

So it is difficult to know what exactly I can do in the pages of *The Wine Advocate* , short of saying, for example, “these young wines from producer x taste great, but anecdotal evidence from collector friends suggests that they may well oxidize”. And if I were to write that, I can readily understand why producers might take exception, as that is hardly a rigorously empirical claim. Any producer about whom I did write such a thing would also likely feel that they were being singled out unfairly if I did not say the same thing for every single producer known to have had, at any time, a premox issue. To give an example of this mentality in a different context, I one wrote an off-hand comment in a note to the effect that “wine x is a great example of a regional wine but does it merit a three-figure price tag?” The producer in question was a little offended, observing that I had not questioned the QPR of Coche’s Bourgogne Blanc or Roumier’s Bourgogne Rouge, which see similar market pricing, so why did I pick on him? That incident made me think more carefully before writing anything that can be seen to be unjustly singling out one producer for criticism.

Another point that has been raised here is that some producers are reluctant to discuss the subject. That is true, but it’s worth fleshing out a bit further. Some producers are certainly in complete denial. And I do understand where they are coming from, in that the prospect of the better part of three years work being ruined by something they don’t really understand is very hard to come to terms with on an emotional level (even if this attitude provokes rage among consumers who have spent their hard-earned money on expensive bottles that do not deliver). Others are convinced they have found a monocausal explanation: i.e., “it’s this cork producer, I’ll show you”, even if the evidence contradicts that. Both attitudes tend to preclude any meaningful discussion.

Then there are producers who will talk about it, but only off the record. Sometimes that’s to avoid offending their neighbors (when I mooted an article on premox and solicited opinions, several producers with good track records declined to be involved for that reason). Or it could be because of family politics: one producer mentioned in this thread as a major premox offender has actually started measuring dissolved oxygen and nitrogen-sparging at the bottling line, but asked me not to write about this, to my astonishment. In such circumstances, I have to respect their wishes even if I find that frustrating.

So there are many complexities here beyond the implicit threat of loosing access to certain domaines (which in itself doesn’t particularly terrify me), and I think the latter point has been exaggerated as an overly simple reason for “silence” on the part of the press. (That may have been the case during the Anne-Claude Leflaive period at Domaine Leflaive, when it was evident that something was seriously wrong, but that was personally before my time and it is hard to think of an equivalent case today). All of the points I have raised make premox a difficult subject for journalists to meaningfully address, without there being any kind of conspiracy of silence. Indeed, I think all the major commentators on white Burgundy are pretty open about the existence of the phenomenon and do bring it up where it is relevant. I certainly discuss it with receptive producers and report on it in TWA as much as I can, and I do try to bring it up with domaines if I am hearing that they are having a lot of issues.
