I am not using reduction in this context to refer to the aromatic artifacts of reductive winemaking as expressed in the glass, but rather to refer to a genuinely reductive élevage that steers the wine in the opposite direction from oxidation. Gross lees consume oxygen, and fine lees release polysaccharides that combine with tannins in a way not dissimilar from that in which anthocyanins combine with tannins in red wine making - hence the enhanced durability of long sur lies aged white wines made with comparatively extractive pressing. Have a look at e.g. Chatonnet et al, “Incidence des Conditions de fermentation de d’élevage des vins blancs secs en barriques”, Sciences des Aliments, 12 (1992). It is a super interesting subject that has not been studied enough.
