> *Replying to :*  
> And what is the trend nowadays? Are we going back to lower yields and more concentration in Champagne? I guess if one looks at it by producer as opposed to “era” one can still find some NVs with higher concentration that are more apt to be aged? Maybe we can crack the 2020s Shampoos code

The short answer is that as a whole, no, we are not: 2018 was one of the largest crops in living memory—see e.g. [https://www.wine-searcher.com/m/2018/08/the-2018-champagne-report](https://www.wine-searcher.com/m/2018/08/the-2018-champagne-report) And we will be living with the last few decades’ choices in terms of clonal selections for some time to come. What we are getting is somewhat riper fruit that’s lower in acidity… and given how soft a lot of 2015-base NV Bruts are tasting as they begin to hit the market, that is not an immensely thrilling proposition if it isn’t accompanied by real depth and concentration. Think flaccid and open-knit rather than textural and vinous.
