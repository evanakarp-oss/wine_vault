> That’s outstanding - William, humor me; what’s your guilty pleasure high octane wine - you know, the way Alfert secretly adores Ovid? I’m genuinely curious…

Ha, that’s a good question!

I would say that my palate is pretty consistent, so there are not going to be too many surprises. I certainly have quite a broad palate within the parameters of what I consider balanced: so while I love the racy acid-driven tension and aromatic range of cooler Burgundy vintages, I can certainly find things more things to admire than some of my friends in the best renditions of much hotter, riper vintages such as 2003. (One question I ponder a lot is why so few 2003s turned out as well as so many 1947s? That is a question Burgundy as a whole is going to have to figure out in the coming decade).

In terms of my personal evolution as a taster, I cut my teeth on very classic stuff: old Bordeaux and old Mosel, getting into Burgundy over time. But I definitely had a few years where I was very interested in “cult” California and the contemporary southern Rhône; I think because the texture and muscle of those wines, especially when young, was such a contrast to what I had formed my palate on. It was familiarity with those high octane, super-ripe wines that led me back to where I had started, which was very classically balanced, mature wines—and I returned to the latter with a much more conscious conception of the seemingly rather abstract qualities of balance, elegance, and finesse precisely because of my intimate experience with wines that didn’t really manifest much of those things.

As for wines that I appreciate a lot that are not really “AFWE-approved”, I’d say I’m a big fan of white Rayas, which when old is an extraordinary gastronomic wine to my palate, pairing well with many of the most delicious, cardiac-arresting components of classical French cooking. But I have many friends who hate that wine. I also really like the wines of Domaine de la Bongran in Viré-Clessé, which generally have a bit of residual sugar as well as botrytis influence, to say nothing of >14% alcohol. (I sometimes reflect that it’s a measure of how impoverished the vocabulary of wine tasting is that, on paper, descriptions of Bongran and Rombauer Chardonnay have a lot in common; yet the wines are totally different in the glass!) To really put myself outside the pale, I’d even happily admit that I wouldn’t turn down a glass of Helen Turley’s 1994 Pahlmeyer Merlot!

But I don’t think anyone looking at my cellar inventory would find anything that’s out of place with what I write; because I try to write about the wines I like; and I buy, within the limits of my means, the wines I praise. I still think that “I own 10 cases of wine from this producer” is a much warmer endorsement than any score I could give.
