> *Replying to :*  
> I think cellaring Beaujolais for twenty plus years is a fool’s errand. It is not meant to be aged, and while you may occasionally come across a decent bottle, the percentage is incredibly low. I buy older cellars, and there are often bottles of Beaujolais hidden and forgotten, and as they are…

As a gross generalization, I suppose that’s true enough. Don’t try to age Dubouef for twenty years. But Bouland, Desvignes, etc, no problem. And the track record of the wines of the 40s, 50s, and 60s proves that if today’s Beaujolais mostly doesn’t age, that because of how it’s made, not because of the region it comes from or the grape from which it’s made.
