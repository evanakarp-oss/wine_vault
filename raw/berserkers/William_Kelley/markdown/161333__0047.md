This wasn’t what Robert was getting at at all, but it is an interesting tangent: adjusting CO2 levels at bottling very precisely is very fashionable right now among enologists in Burgundy (and I would guess elsewhere) and I know that a number of them really encourage their clients to do this for both red and white wines. There is quite sophisticated equipment for it available: e.g. [http://michaelpaetzold.com/fr/produits/carbonication-décarbonication](http://michaelpaetzold.com/fr/produits/carbonication-d%C3%A9carbonication) So this is not just about retaining gas from fermentation but to do with adding it. The idea is to bring “freshness” to the wine without adding so much that tannin / structure are emphasized. Given that my main interest is in wines made to age 20+ years, it has typically struck me as a bit unnecessary; and despite the enologists’ best efforts I find quite a few of these gassed red wines taste unnecessarily firm on release from the CO2 addition (of course, it doesn’t help when the same enologists are also recommending producers use extraction enzymes during the fermentation).
