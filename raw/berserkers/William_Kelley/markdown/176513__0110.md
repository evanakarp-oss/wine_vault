> *Replying to :*  
> Which leads me to more questions; some of which I don’t expect answers. The report seems to indicate that all Burgundy is good, or even very good. 2020 seems like a solid vintage, but really? Every producer is awesome? What about producers not in the report? Some, I expect, didn’t get in for…

Well, consider the amount of time it takes to visit 100 domaines and write up the ensuing tasting notes. Obviously, that simply isn’t possible. So there has to be a selection. In that regard, I divide our coverage of the Côte d’Or up into two big reports. The end-of-year report focusses on barrel tastings of wines that are sold as futures, so mainly the more prominent villages of the Côte d’Or (though increasingly domaines are moving away from EP sales and showing finished wines in bottle, which I much prefer). At the same time, I try to look at a good sample of the most recently bottled vintage to see how that has turned out. Then, in Spring, I cover less well known producers, often from less famous villages, along with the Côte Chalonnaise and Hautes-Côtes. This is more of a “discovery” report, ranging widely to find interesting new producers.

Within these two reports, the selection is necessarily, in a sense, arbitrary, in that it’s entirely at my discretion and reflects on the one hand, what interests me and appeals to my palate, and on the other, a desire to offer a broad overview of the vintage along the Côte d’Or as interpreted by producers operating in different stylistic registers. I do dare to break with some of my colleagues/competitors in not always visiting *all* the producers that received opinion considers “reference points”, because I try to prioritize discovering new estates, and visiting the exact same cast of producers every year, without any time to discover new estates, would be tedious. And yes, I am definitely trying to find good wine, not bad, for our readers; and so the reports are heavily skewed towards the best, not the worst, that I think the region has to offer. If you would like a selection of utterly revolting wines, I could supply one, and sometimes just to remind myself of what the average uninterested consider will encounter, I buy a few bottles of “Bourgogne Rouge” at a local supermarket. It is not the stuff that dreams are made of.

Then, beyond then, there are logistical accidents: a producer forgets an appointment, or gets sick, or their neighbor’s tractor breaks and they have to treat their vineyards for them, or whatever, that normally eliminate a small handful of domaines that I intended to visit from any given report.
