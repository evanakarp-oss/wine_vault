I hesitated to contribute to this thread, for obvious reasons, and many of the questions raised in this thread are quite familiar. But I wanted to give another perspective but explaining what a 100-point score I gave recently “means”; because what the score means—beyond the fact that the wine is movingly brilliant and situated among the very best of its peer group—varies wine by wine.

The example I wanted to pick is Cédric Bouchard’s 2008 Les Ursules. For me, giving that wine 100 points earlier this year, in addition to capturing my emotions on tasting the wine and how I feel it is situated in the broader context of sparkling wine, hopefully conveys a number of things. Mostly importantly, I hope it emphasizes that there are no glass ceilings limiting how Champagne is scored in The Wine Advocate: this is a Blanc de Noirs rather than a blend; from an unprestigious region that’s at the bottom of the echelle des crus; made by a grower who started a new label and not a Grande Marque with a long history. If any producers were thinking, “we’ll never get recognition for what we’re doing, given who we are and where we make wine”, I hope this review dispels that notion. The positive side of the 100-point system, in other words, is that it can disrupt hierarchies based on “received opinion” and old classifications that tend to stifle so-called “lesser” appellations or new producers. Beyond this important statement, this particular score also affirms that I think Les Usules is as good any of Cédric’s wines (is the hierarchical mentality so ever-present in the wine world that we tend to look for “grands crus” even when there are none?) even if La Bolorée and Creux d’Enfer are more caught after; and it also expresses my conviction that his wines do age—but like still wines rather than like Champagnes (this is a subject we have gotten into before so I won’t expand on the point and derail the thread).

I discussed all this with Cédric the other day when I went to taste his new releases, and he had understood all that, but also had his own point of view. He recalled how, back in the early years of his project, he was ridiculed by his neighbors and even family members: “there’s grass growing in your vineyards, you’re not taking care of them, you should apply some herbicides”; “you didn’t make the yield this year” (the yield limits in Champagne are generally taken as a target, “faire le rendement”, to attain the maximum yield, having no pejorative connotations). For him, receiving 100/100 for a wine from those early years was, as he put it, the final vindication from those close-to-home critiques. Incidentally, even Lalou Bize-Leroy said the same last month with regards to Robert Parker’s high scores for her wines back in the 1990s.

In this case, I was glad to be able to give Cédric this recognition, and indeed amazed that it remained for me to do it. And I hope others will agree that, employed this way, the highest of all scores can be meaningful and more than simply something that enflames the market.
