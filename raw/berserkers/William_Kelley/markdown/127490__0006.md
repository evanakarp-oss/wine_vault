Karl:

I’m just extrapolating from experience. Having drunk a bunch of old claret, they often tasted thin and metallic when popped and poured; on exposure to a decent amount of oxygen they typically fleshed out, the fruit popped and they became fantastic wines. I should add that it took a bit of courage to do that given all the stuff out there about ‘be sure not to decant for too long, old wines are fragile etc’. But that is, in fact, nonsense unless we are talking about genuinely old wines (in Bordeaux, 1930s or before or very weak more recent vintages). The metallic taste itself is, I assume, to do with sulphur chemistry, but if I attempt to offer a more detailed explanation I am sure I will be corrected.

I was referring specifically to the '70 Pape Clement with the red-fruity remark. While it’s a pretty high-acid vintage (especially by today’s standards) and the wines are generally what I would call high-toned, the comment doesn’t generalize well. The 1970 Haut Bailly, for example, from not so far away, is pretty black fruity.

By double decant I just mean decant the wine, clean the bottle and pour the wine back into the bottle. That worked well, but the bottom line is that this wine can withstand, and improve by being exposed to, a big hit of oxygen!
