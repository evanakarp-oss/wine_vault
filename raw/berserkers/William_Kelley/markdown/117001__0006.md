This raises a more general question about how strongly longevity and hierarchy correlate in Burgundy. While there obviously is a correlation, and a pretty strong one too, I would suggest that it is sometimes assumed too uncritically. On the one hand, it’s easy to imagine a Clos St Jaques or Cros Parantoux having a longer life expectancy than many renditions of Latricieres or Charmes Chambertin; and I can even think of Vosne Beaux Monts that seem likely to outlive Richebourgs of the same vintage. On the other, I think there must be few renditions of Vosne Chaumes that are likely to outlive, say, Mugneret Gibourg’s Vosne AOC.

In addition to exceptions to the rule, it’s certainly the case—in some cellars and vintages—that the village wines don’t take *that* much less time to really open up than the 1ers, and 1ers don’t take that much less time to open up than the grand crus. That is not to say that the higher classifications will not carry on going strong when the village wines have begun to run out of gas, but rather to suggest that the best village wines are no more good to go at age five than 1er or grand crus are.

Obviously, wine making is going to be a hugely important factor in this, which brings us back to the original question, to which the answer, I think, is that it would be possible to make an extremely structured, long-lived Bourgogne, but that doing so would somewhat defeat the purpose of Bourgogne and is unlikely to be justified or facilitated by the intrinsic character of the site.
