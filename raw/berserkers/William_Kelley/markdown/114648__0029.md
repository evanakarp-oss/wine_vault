This is an endlessly interesting subject.

One question raised by Jasper’s article is whether ostensibly premoxed wines that ‘recover’ and freshen up with decanting, even improving in color, ever show as well as indisputably correct bottles. I have had mid-1990s Lafon bottles that have certainly improved and brightened up with decanting, but never hit the heights of the great vintages of the 1970s and 1980s that I have encountered.

Another question is whether any one else with deep experience agrees with Jasper’s contention that the 1996 white Burgundies have recovered from premox. In fact, I was expecting this to excite more controversy. The tiny sample of bottles I’ve had lately—with their sherry-and-chicken broth aromatics, their deeply oxidized colors and their weirdly bleached corks—show no signs of ever being fit for human consumption. Of course, perhaps the argument is that some 1996s (like the latter) really are oxidized, whereas others were just going through a ‘phase’… But I would be surprised if wines like the 1996 Lafon Meursaults or the 1996 Etienne Sauzet Pulignys ever come back from the place to which they appear to have gone.

The Dubourdieu and Lavigne article also begs a number of questions, most obviously concerning two of their recommendations, 1) that the must be protected by SO2 and inert gas, and 2) that alcoholic and malolactic fermentation proceed rapidly. Neither of these practices were consistently part of pre-premox Burgundy winemaking as I understand it, where long, slow fermentation and malo were often the norm (at least in cold cellars, so perhaps not in Puligny), and where no inert gas was available to ‘protect’ the must. And yet, many of the wines made in that era are cruising along beautifully and look and taste more youthful than wines made as many as two decades later.

But multi-factorial problems are always going to be hard to solve.
