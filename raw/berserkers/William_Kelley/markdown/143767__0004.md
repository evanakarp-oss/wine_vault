The Raveneau family’s vines in Monts Mains would have been 20 years old in 2012, and along with their vines in Fôret they’re the domaine’s youngest 1er cru vines.

2012 is an absolutely exceptional Raveneau vintage, and a very fine Chablis vintage indeed overall, so what you are tasting is not the year’s limitations.

While it’s true that Raveneau makes some of the most concentrated, textural wines in Chablis, I’d look for those qualities in the Butteaux, Montée de Tonnerre and three Grand Cru bottlings rather than the Vaillons, Fôret and Monts Mains.

That said, I’d expect more mid-palate amplitude from the 2012 Monts Mains in a few more years. Now is a good time to start looking at 2005s and earlier vintages - though of course that is rarely possible off restaurant wine lists.

Resembling both Louis Michel and Laurent Tribut simultaneously is something of a feat, as the former picks early and vinifies in stainless steel, whereas the latter picks late and ages in wood. Which goes to show how small the stylistic bandwidth is in Chablis, at the end of the day, by comparison with other wine regions.
