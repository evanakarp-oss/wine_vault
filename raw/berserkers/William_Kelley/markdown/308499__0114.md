Hi David,

In fact, inerted presses were tried out a bit in the 80s/90s and subsequently abandoned, by Lafon among others. So people have been thinking about this! And while riper fruit from sunnier vintages gives you more polyphenoloxidase, if you let your must brown before barreling down I am not sure how serious this really is.

What I think people were, and in some cases still are, doing, was inhibiting the browning process by sulfiting heavily in the press pan and settling tank, and sometimes blanketing with dry ice too.

As for the question of glutathione, have a look at the Dubourdieu paper, “Role du glutathion sur l’evolution des vins blancs secs” (2003), where he demonstrates it is absorbed during primary fermentation then gradually re-released during sur lie élevage in a reductive environment. Which takes us back to the interest in turbid musts + long élevage!

Also, we have much less botrytis now than 20 years ago because the cycle is earlier and we harvest earlier in the year. 2013 was the last vintage with appreciable botrytis in Côte de Beaune whites.
