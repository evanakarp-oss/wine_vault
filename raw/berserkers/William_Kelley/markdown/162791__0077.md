> That’s a great question. Flavors do evolve, and regardless of the ability to measure it in a lab most winemakers have their own very subjective beliefs as to what “physiological ripeness” is. Flavors do evolve up through a spectrum, in addition to gaining sugars in the fruit. As acids drop out…

Nicely put! Ripeness is not a moment but a continuum…

And to Dennis’ point, beyond phenolic maturity and sugar accumulation, there are other contingent factors that come into play. This year in Burgundy, many vines are very stressed. From a small, very high-altitude parcel where I am making 100 liters of wine (!), the vines were already shutting down, with leaves yellowing and dropping. I’d say 90% of seeds were fully brown, and the rest weren’t bitter. Stem lignification was pretty good. Flavors were vibrant and really “popped”. I could have waited a few days more but a big heat spike was coming, with warm nights, so I picked August 20th without taking any analyses. Numbers came in at 12.6% potential abv, ph 3.20, 1.7 grams malic and 8.2 grams tartaric. I’m very happy with that. By contrast, I have another small plot that I won’t pick until Friday, where conditions are very different. At the end of the day, these decisions end up being very intuitive.
