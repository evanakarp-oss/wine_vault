> *Replying to :*  
> Never tasted Rayas 1978, a summit !

It is pretty profound, but I think many of the older Louis Reynaud-era wines can be as good or better. Rayas from the '50s and '60s might be my desert island red wine (and I write this as someone who lives in Burgundy for much of the year). Jacques Reynaud’s wines were a bit more muscular and extracted: the 1995 is still opaque and impenetrable, and the 1989, my vintage, is still just at the beginning of its interesting life. My impression is that the Louis Reynaud wines were a bit more elegant: just as concentrated, but a bit less blocky and chunky if that makes sense. It will be interesting to see what Emmanuel’s wines do with 40 years on the clock…

Regarding Rayas, this video is pretty exceptional for Francophone readers… [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Vqqg_NRLOEs](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Vqqg_NRLOEs) If I did not have too many things on my plate I’d try to add English subtitles, but for a 26-minute video that’s quite an undertaking. You will note that Reynaud uses whole bunches but crushes the fruit very thoroughly, and that fermentations take place with a submerged cap plus pump-overs… I wonder how much techniques have evolved since Louis’ and Jacques’ eras?
