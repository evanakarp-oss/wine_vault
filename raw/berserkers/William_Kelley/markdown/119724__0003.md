I have quite a bit of experience with these wines in both their older and more recent incarnations, and would say that you can buy Haut Bailly with confidence.

Their vintages 2010 and 2009 were certainly a bit denser and riper than is the norm, but that is true pretty much across the board in all Bordeaux, including addresses which were not looking to push the boundaries, such as Montrose or VCC. New oak percentages range between 50% and 65%, which is pretty typical these days—a similar or even a bit lower than the numbers you find at Montrose and VCC to stick with those examples. A percentage of the production goes through malo in barrel, for what that’s worth, though that’s a complex subject. And Veronique Sanders, who I admire very much, has a classical palate and sensibility. A few years ago she presented a tasting from my former tasting group in Oxford, and we were very much on the same page in our appreciation of the magnum of 1970 Pape Clement we all enjoyed over dinner afterwards, which was pretty far from being a modernist Graves!

If you’re looking for classically-proportioned vintages, the 1998 is a very undervalued maturing example (probably a decade from really expatiating), and one of Veronique’s favorites. The 2006 is very creditable and also a bit overlooked. And the 2008 is just terrific, with lots of tension and vibrancy. 2012 is very nice too, but cut from more open-knit, supple cloth, and likely to give pleasure just a bit earlier than the very concentrated, long-haul 2008.
