The question of individual commune identifies is an interesting one. Clearly, some communes do have some unifying features: the high percentage of active limestone in Chambolle, for example, or the predominantly south-easterly exposition of Puligny-Montrachet. But clearly, every commune also comprehends significant variation in geology / soil hydrology and altitude, as well as frequently significant variation in exposition and air-drainage. What does Meursault Les Vireuils Dessus, east-north-east facing and over 300m in elevation, with poor rocky soils below the tree line, having in common with Meursault Les Pellans, south-east facing, below 200m in elevation, on the rich soils at the bottom of Meursault Charmes? Doesn’t Vireuils Dessus have more in common with the high altitude parts of Puligny than with Meursault Les Pellans?

In the past, wines from a commune, produced by winemakers from the commune, were often made using the same methods. Deep, cold cellars in Meursault, where the water table is low, favored long fermentation and sur lie élevage in barrel; in Puligny, the shallow water table precluded that, favoring shorter élevage in larger vessels. If you needed winemaking advice to solve a problem in Puligny fifty or sixty years ago, you might ask a member of the Virot family; whereas in Meursault you might have asked René Lafon. That naturally made for more of a family resemblance between the wines of a village (even though there was clearly always more bandwidth than the caricatures allow: did François Jobard’s chiseled, austere wines ever fit the stereotype of buttery Meursault?). And when the négociants blended, they aimed to make blends that met consumer expectations for each appellation: Meursault, after all, is a brand as well as a place.

Today, as I’ve argued here before, these unifying considerations have broken down. With air conditioning and humidity control you can do a long élevage in Puligny; and with heaters in your cold cellar in Meursault, you can force your wine through alcoholic and malolactic fermentation, then filter it for bottling within a year. If you need advice, you call a consulting winemaker who may not even have come from the region. At the same time, many lieux-dits that were often blended are now bottled separately: combine Vireuils Dessus and Pellans in a blend and you might have a more “classic” Meursault; bottle them separately, and you have two very different wines. It is not that site differences have been effaced: on the contrary, micro-bottlings have multiplied the “expressions of terroir” kaleidoscopically.

All of this means that, tasting blind, it is much harder to identify the commune than it is with wines from thirty years ago. When I blind taste for fun, I try to guess the producer **and** the vineyard. (And sometimes, the consultant oenologist). But honestly, I find blind tasting more interesting and useful for the surprises that occur when one gets it wrong, which are an important heuristic for when I taste knowing what’s in the glass, as opposed to the satisfaction of getting it right. I loved Jim Anderson’s comments on blind tasting, by the way, everyone should read that closely.
