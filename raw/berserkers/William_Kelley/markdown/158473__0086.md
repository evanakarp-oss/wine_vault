> *Replying to :*  
> William: To me the single most important factor, which I wish the critics would insist on knowing from every white burgundy producer whose wines they review, is the amount of free SO2 at bottling and the related number of total SO2 (which helps insure a stable level of free SO2.) I have…

I, too, have discussed this with David and I am aware of his strongly-held views. And I can certainly ask such questions more frequently (two more data points for you, off the top of my head: PY Colin told me that he bottled his 2015s with 55 ppm free, and Ballot Millot aim for around 40 ppm - both using natural cork). But my issue here is that the answers may be unreliable, for two reasons. One, that numbers of this kind are often treated as “advisory” in Burgundy, and may reflect, e.g. a sample taken from a wine prepared for bottling before the floating cap of a variable capacity tank was removed to take a quick sample for a visiting importer (I have seen this happen, to my horror). Add the different qualities of bottling lines in use in Burgundy, and what actually makes it into the bottle may vary widely from what I am told. Two, as soon as producers perceive that I am systematically asking the question, I will be given the answer that it is thought that I want to hear: again, to give an example, today almost no one on the Côte will admit to doing battonage; yet if you look around in the wineries, the dodines for stirring the lees are even sometimes still dripping.

The more interesting thing would be to run numbers on wines from bottle. Perhaps at your next dinner you might take ETS samples of all the wines?

But even if oxidation is correlated with low sulfur, I am not sure that the inverse is necessarily true. I am looking at the numbers on a wine I know well from a top CdB producer from 2012 that had 66 ppm total and 25 ppm free SO2 at bottling and it is evolving superbly. And I have some numbers on entry level wines from one of the producers on your list as a very much below-average premox culprit where free SO2s are below 15 under natural cork - yet I did not experience or even hear of any oxidation in those wines. (I don’t have permission to share the numbers so I can’t say which producer, so you will have to take it on faith or not, sorry).

This is not to say that free SO2 is not an important aspect of the problem, but if there really were such an obvious single culprit and corresponding silver bullet, surely the issue would have been solved by now. What’s more, if the variability of cork and its impact on free SO2 is the main cause of premox, why didn’t it happen systematically before 1995?
