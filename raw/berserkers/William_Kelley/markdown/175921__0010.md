If you want the actual details, here’s the relevant expert from my article:
> Olivier’s idea to plant at a very high density derived from a variety of sources. In 1990, Lamy’s father had planted two small parcels of Saint-Aubin 1er Cru En Remilly at a density of 14,000 vines per hectare—simply as a way to fit more vines into an awkwardly shaped block. He liked the resulting grapes and planted other parcels at higher density in the 1990s. Those additional vines, however, made for a lot of additional work at pruning time; so, by 2001, Olivier was keen to test just how much of a difference, if any, planting at higher density made: he decided to push things to extremes, planting a new block at 30,000 vines per hectare in Derrière Chez Edouard, as well as co-planting new young vines between existing mature vines. His neighbors were initially suspicious, thinking his intention was to produce more volume; but their qualms were calmed when they realized that Lamy’s yields in the new plantings actually fell.> Olivier Lamy in one of his high-density vineyards> Simultaneously, Lamy was keen to improve the quality of his Chardonnay vine genetics in Criots-Bâtard-Montrachet and Puligny-Montrachet Les Trémblots. Uprooting and replanting was one option, and in the Criots, he did indeed rip up some vines that produced less interesting fruit. But he soon decided to try a different approach, co-planting young, high-quality Chardonnay plants in between the existing vines in order to improve the average quality from the parcel. Initially, his neighbors were once again skeptical, wondering if he was trying to complicate the sale of the Criots-Bâtard-Montrachet, where he was only a sharecropper at the time, by exceeding the planting densities specified in the appellation’s> cahier des charges> . Today, however, the results speak for themselves.> Beyond these more practical considerations, Lamy was also aware that, before phylloxera, Burgundian vineyards were often planted at densities exceeding 20,000 vines per hectare. It was only when the necessity of grafting> vitis vinifera> onto phylloxera-resistant American rootstocks imposed a comprehensive restructuring of the region’s vineyards that vines were planted in rows, with trellising wires, at densities of 10,000 to 12,000 vines per hectare. Until then, Burgundy had been a hold-out for the old system of “provinage,” whereby new vines were propagated by burying a shoot from the parent vine and waiting for it to take root. In these vineyards, vines grew in a disorderly pattern at very high densities, necessitating manual cultivation, with each vine trained to its own supporting stake (these stakes were known as “paisseaux” in Burgundy, the equivalent of the “échalas” of the Northern Rhône). Lamy wondered if the incredible concentration and longevity of pre-phylloxera Burgundies owed anything to these high-density plantings.
