> As one relatively new to red burgundy appreciation, how do Chassagne reds compare stylistically to other villages? FWIW, I tend to put Chambolle and Volnay on the smoother, richer end of things and Pommard on the other sterner, firmer side. (I’m sure such a broad generalization has numerous…

In the 20th century, they have tended to be quite chunky, rich, dark fruited and somewhat austere, rustic wines; but it is the exceptions to this rule that are the interesting wines!

Chassagne has some superb sites for the production of authoritative but quite elegant red wine, so the reason for the wines’ historic rusticity has more to do with reds made from some of the lesser sites; and, even more so, poor quality selections of Pinot Noir genetics. Marsannay, Pernand and Santenay were the sources of very productive selections of Pinot in the later 19th century, and Chassagne’s proximity to Santenay meant that many growers adopted these fatter-berried, higher yielding selections. In the 1970s, very productive SO4 rootstocks came into the picture, too. These selections’ tendency to produce huge yields is one reason Chassagne growers traditionally pruned in cordon rather than Guyot - as a way to mitigate the yields. Here’s a photo of the grapes that such selections produce that I took during the 2020 harvest:

[Chassagne Pinot Noir](https://flic.kr/p/2mL3LiZ) by [WilliamGFKelley](https://www.flickr.com/photos/55246154@N07/) , on Flickr

Grapes like this produce wine that’s somewhat herbaceous and rustic, with chewy tannins. And by the looks of it, these vines seemed to me to be around forty years old, so vine age only does so much in terms of mitigating productivity. Of course, the vine’s phenotypic expression can be modulated by viticulture: increase the height of the trellising and don’t hedge, and clusters will elongate and berries become smaller. But if you farm using herbicide, lots of nitrogen fertilizer, and regular 1.10 meter trimming, you are not going to have raw materials conducive to producing great wine. And this would and does hold just as true of vines like these growing in a top Vosne-Romanée 1er cru as it does in Chassagne.

One upside to the decline of Chassagne red is that the least suitable sites and worst quality vine material has tended to be ripped out first, leaving us with the cream that floated to the top. Sites such as Ramonet’s Clos de la Boudriotte are planted with quite fine selections of Pinot Noir and it’s not surprising that the wines are more elegant, perfumed, and sensual. It is therefore the appellation’s best examples that give us a sense of what is possible with Chassagne red (I’d be tempted to agree with Rodier, who thought them the most Côte de Nuits-like of the Côte de Beaune’s reds, for what little such generalizations are worth), rather than the average.
