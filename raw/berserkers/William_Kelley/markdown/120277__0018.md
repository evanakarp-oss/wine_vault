I thought it was a nicely done piece all things considered. Not as good as her recent Santa Cruz Mountains piece, but still solid.

One might object, as has been mentioned, that Screaming Eagle is not really a hillside property. Neither, for that matter, is Araujo’s Eisele vineyard. Hard not to notice that when one visits.

I also wonder how accurately the heady atmosphere of the period in which the ‘cult’ cabs emerged has been rendered. I was not there to experience it, but one picks things up.  According to the accounts I’ve heard, time was when, say, Spottswoode, Dunn and Togni were almost part of the cult cab conversation. When you put a bottle down on the table heads turned. Then there were what one might call the ‘busted flushes’, like Behrens and Hitchcock, Sloan etc than never really made good the early hype, but looked as if they might at one point.

So to make out that ‘cult’ cabs only ever meant Harlan, Screaming Eagle and a handful of others is teleological, limiting what could be a fascinating essay in anthropology and cultural history.
