> *Replying to :*  
> If “grip” or “searing levels of extract” or whatever you want to call it means that these white wines are meant to age, how does that interact with premox? Does that level of structure mean premox is less likely, or is there a risk that these white wines will not be able to get the necessary age?…

Flavenoids etc in the skins are indeed antioxidants. An approach to pressing that extracts from the skins also tends to result in turbid musts with lots of suspended solids, which contribute to copious lees and more reductive conditions in barrel. Pressing this way takes time, so there is also plenty of opportunity for the juice oxidation of which Otto speaks (much is made of juice oxidation, but it’s actually quite difficult to avoid, it happens naturally is you don’t make special efforts to stop it) before the wine goes to barrel for fermentation. When wines made this way from good grapes are bottled without high levels of dissolved oxygen, with correct sulfur levels, and closed with a good closure, I believe that they will not be susceptible to premox. Sadly, all of the pieces of this puzzle very seldom align.

Equally, it’s important to emphasize that huge levels of extract are not in themselves a guarantor of longevity, just as huge levels of tannin are not in red wine.
