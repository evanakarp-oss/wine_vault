> *Replying to :*  
> Thanks for the notes. For me, the Bouley wines are markedly better from 2012 on. I also think that despite recent hype for the fremiers, the volnay caillerets is particularly special. Alex

Having done a big vertical with Thomas earlier this year, I’m not sure I agree that there was a huge shift in 2012. Progress has been incremental, year on year, since Thomas took over in the early 2000s, and if I had to single out one step change, I’d point to the difference between 2008 and 2010, which is largely to do with better sanitation for used barrels. I do agree, however, that what Thomas did in the hail vintages of 2012, 2013 and 2014 is very impressive! Since then, he has extended élevage and further refined cooperage choices, so the progress continues. Thomas himself considers 2010, 2015 and 2019 to be the best he has made to date, FWIW.

I’ve tried to highlight how good Fremiers is lately, because Rugiens and Caillerets both tended to get all the love due to name recognition, whereas I think Fremiers is, for Thomas and given the locations of his holdings, the better wine: it’s deeper, more complete, and more multidimensional. His vines in Caillerets were planted in 2004 vs Fermiers in 1970, which makes a difference, too (though at this level of viticulture, vine age matters less I think). Caillerets tends to be the ripest / highest alcohol wine in the cellar. But the location of the parcels is the key: his Rugiens is right at the top of the vineyard to the north, with white soils and a steep slope, producing an ethereal, linear wine that’s very far from the stereotype of Rugiens-Bas with its red soils and very full, muscular wines; and his Caillerets is similarly situated, by the road to the north, not from the sector near the Clos des 60 Ouvrées which has redder, richer soils and makes the most complete wines in this big climat. That’s not a criticism of those wines, I hasten to add, as I love them both: but when you compare those parcels with his plot of Fremiers, it makes complete sense that they should be different in the ways that they are, and one could say, I think, that his Fremiers is “more Rugiens” than his Rugiens.

The real values in the range, however, are certainly the Hautes-Côtes (from a beautiful enclave on the plateau just above Volnay, surrounded by trees) and the Volnay Clos de la Cave, and with regional and communal bottlings at that level we are already spoiled.
