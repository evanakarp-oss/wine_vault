> *Replying to :*  
> I wonder if we are going to start seeing oxidized coche with the regime change. Seems to have far less sulfur / matchstick on the nose now.

Raphael is still pressing like his father, with lots of dry extract in the wines, and the élevage is just as detail-oriented, so is the bottling. I am not too concerned on that front.

I think the idea that overt reduction is the key to avoiding premox is a blind alley. Have seen plenty of white Burgundies go from reduced to oxidized with nothing in between, and the great wines of René Lafon and plenty of others were not reduced and aged very well.

Threads on premox tend to go off on tangents, often devolving into debates about screw caps, with everyone in search of one single cause, when the reality is complex and multifactorial but not so mysterious. Picking date, fruit quality, pressing (how hard, how long), élevage (how much lees, how much stirring and when, how much new oak, potential use of clarification enzymes, cellar temperature, barrel topping, sulfur additions and other factors), bottling (sulfur levels, dissolved oxygen, bottling practices) and closures are all part of a complex system. Some of the explanations are to do with technical mistakes (e.g. picking up too much dissolved oxygen in racking tanks and bottling lines) but others are to do with style and intention: no one really wants to wait a decade on Bourgogne Blanc and village wines and two for premiers crus, and producers understand this. Moreover, in the premox era our collective palate is changing: most of us would find Pierre Ramonet’s wines of the '70s and '80s far too austere and structural if we went back in time to taste them from barrel today.
