I think the two elements of Claude Dugat’s approach that could be described as “modern” were (I use the past tense because his children are now directing the estate and have made subtle changes) his use of large percentages of new oak and his practice of avoiding (if I am not mistaken) racking his wines of their original lees until their assembly in tank for bottling.

In all other respect, Dugat’s winemaking was extremely traditional. In the vineyard, old selection massale plants, meticulous viticulture (like his cousin’s vines, Dugat’s vines are immediately recognizable by virtue of the quality of the farming) and moderate yields. In the cuverie and cellar, 100% destemming (for which there is in fact a long tradition in Burgundy - it being done by hand with wooden grills back in the day), fermentation without a cold soak in cement tanks, often with light chaptalization (something the children have stopped), twice-daily punch downs, two winters’ élevage and bottling without fining and filtration.

The wines and the family are warmly respected in France and within Burgundy, and I have always wondered why they were controversial in the USA. Did their former importer, Kacher, pick the most overtly oaky barrels for export? Did the critical acclaim they received from some quarters provoke a reaction in others? Or is it simply that the wines need quite a bit more bottle age than many of their neighbors’? Perhaps it’s simply a divisive style to the Anglo-Saxon palate. What I can say is that you’d have derived a lot more pleasure, Dennis, from the 2011 right now than the 2010. In terms of ‘top vintages’, the 2002 is the youngest that is really drinking at its best.

I recently drank a bottle of Claude Dugat over lunch with Jean-François Coche, and he pronounced it ‘excellent: the archetype of Gevrey-Chambertin’. One man’s opinion, but one that I’d second.

Also, as JW notes, the prices are some of the region’s most reasonable: the village Gevrey is 30-35 EUR from the Domaine.
